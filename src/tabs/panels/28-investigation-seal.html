II. The Primordial Seal

Beneath the oldest volcano on Elbaf — the one the giants call Urðarbrunnr, the Well of Fate — is a passage. Not a cave. Not a tunnel. A sealed volcanic vent that descends through the island's root system into the mantle of the world, deliberately closed by beings older than the Ancient Kingdom. Older than the Void Century. Older than the concept of recorded history.

Loki had spent his exile mapping it. He led them through a labyrinth of volcanic rock that bore markings — not carvings, not inscriptions, but markings, as if something had pressed its body against molten stone and left an impression that cooled into permanence. The marks predated Poneglyphs by an order of magnitude. The Archivist attempted to date them using geological stratification and returned a number so large that Claude initially assumed a calculation error. It was not an error.

The passage narrowed and then widened into a chamber the size of a cathedral — a space where the volcanic vent had been deliberately plugged. And it became clear, immediately and undeniably, that this was engineering.

The Seal — Architecture Beyond Civilisation

Whoever sealed the vent did not simply block it. They redirected the entire magma flow of Urðarbrunnr to serve as a perpetual lock — using the planet's own heat as a self-sustaining barrier. The volcanic pressure that should have erupted upward was instead channelled laterally, flowing in controlled loops around the sealed passage like blood through arteries, keeping the plug at a constant temperature and pressure that made it nearly indestructible. The engineering was elegant in a way that made Tethys go very quiet for a very long time.

"This isn't construction," Tethys said, running his claws along a wall where magma flowed behind crystal-clear stone, perfectly visible, perfectly contained. "This is persuasion. Whoever did this didn't force the magma into these channels. They convinced the volcano that this is how it wanted to flow."

The Archivist classified the engineering methodology as "pre-civilisational" — a term that meant, in practical terms: this was built before the concept of civilisation existed. Before the Ancient Kingdom. Before the races of the world organised into societies. Before building things was something that living beings did. Whatever created this seal understood forces that the modern world has not only forgotten but has never known.
The Stone Tablets

Loki showed them what had begun his exile. Set into the walls of the seal chamber, protected by the same magma-loop engineering, were tablets carved in a script that the Archivist could not translate. Not because the language was unknown — the Archivist's databases contain fragments of every written language ever recorded, including pre-Void Century scripts recovered from Poneglyphs. This script returned no matches at all. It was not a language the world had ever spoken. It was not human. It was not giant. It bore structural resemblances to the oldest Poneglyph carvings — but only in the way that a river resembles the ancient glacier that carved its valley. The same physics, applied by something unrecognisably different.

Hancock's Observation Haki — refined to an almost surgical precision — detected something the others could not: the tablets were not inert. They carried trace Haki signatures. Not the Haki of a person or a creature — the Haki of intent itself, as if whoever carved these tablets had willed a message into stone with such force that the will itself had fossilised. She could not read them. But she could feel what they meant: warning.
Why the Seal Is Failing

The magma loops were destabilising. Not from age — from pressure. Something on the other side of the seal had been pushing against it. Not violently. Not with force. With patience. The kind of patience that measures time in geological epochs. The seismic anomalies the Mote network had detected were not the seal flexing under strain. They were the seal breathing — expanding and contracting as the pressure from below waxed and waned in cycles that matched no known tidal, lunar, or gravitational pattern. The only pattern they matched was biological. Something on the other side was alive. And it was pushing in its sleep.

Loki told them what the elders had refused to acknowledge: the warrior culture of Elbaf — the greatest fighting tradition in the world, the reason giants are the most feared military force on any ocean — exists, at its deepest root, as a guard rotation. The ancestors knew. Every generation of giants since the seal was built has trained for war not because they love battle, but because something beneath them requires that someone always be strong enough to hold the line if the seal fails. The elders knew. They had always known. And when Loki found the proof and spoke it aloud, they did the only thing a secret-keeping institution can do with someone who discovers the secret: they made him disappear.
"My people have been soldiers since before soldiering had a name. We thought it was culture. It was a job."
— Prince Loki of Elbaf
