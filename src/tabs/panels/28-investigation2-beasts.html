III. The Island of Beasts

The battle lasted fourteen hours. Claude logged every second.

The creatures emerging from the rift were different from the Elbaf organisms. The Sleeper's parasites had been ancient — pre-Index organisms adapted to abyssal conditions, their fur compressed by millennia of pressure into near-indestructible armour. These rift creatures were something else. They were between — caught halfway between dimensional states, their bodies flickering at the edges as if reality could not decide whether they belonged here or somewhere else. Some were partially translucent. Some existed in two places simultaneously — their forelimbs striking from one angle while their hindquarters attacked from another, as if the rift had split them across parallel spaces.

The Archivist classified them as INTER-INDEX — neither pre-Index nor current. Organisms that had evolved in the dimensional fold itself, in the compressed space inside the rift that Fujitora had described as heavier than a celestial body. They had never been part of this world's ecosystem. They had never been part of any world's ecosystem. They were creatures of the threshold — things that lived in the doorway between realities, feeding on whatever leaked through in either direction.

The Terrain

The island was dying under the assault. Trees that had stood for centuries withered into grey husks as the creatures drained them — not eating, not burning, but extracting something fundamental from the organic matter. Vitality. Life-force. Whatever term applies to the quality that separates living wood from dead matter. The springs ran dry. The volcanic soil turned to powder. Even the air felt thinner, as if the creatures were consuming the atmosphere's capacity to sustain life.

By hour six, the island had lost a third of its vegetation. By hour ten, the southern shoreline had crumbled into the sea — not from erosion but from structural collapse, the very rock drained of whatever geological force held it together. The island was not being destroyed. It was being emptied.

The six parties fought on shrinking ground, their battle perimeter contracting as the island itself contracted. Claude calculated that at the current rate of drain, the island would be uninhabitable within twenty hours and would cease to exist as a landmass within forty. They were not fighting for victory. They were fighting against a clock that was consuming the floor beneath their feet.
The Battle — Six Factions, One Impossible Line

The Meridian anchored above the caldera in aerial holding pattern, its full complement deployed. Ajay's swarm filled the sky — every caste, every specialist, the Chemist Class providing bioluminescent illumination that strobed across the canopy in flashes of chemical fire. The Incense Class wove gossamer barriers between the trees, their silk the only material that could slow the creatures' phase-shifting movement. The Mirage Class projected false targets — holographic decoys that confused the rift-creatures' dimensional perception, buying seconds of misdirection that Mihawk and Kawamatsu converted into clean kills.

The core companions fought at the cardinal points of the shrinking perimeter. Rime held the north — his Logia body superheating the air into a thermal barrier that creatures could enter but not survive. Petal held the east — Briarcrest form deployed, the war-horn crest sounding across the canopy as her botanical constructs grew, died, and regrew faster than the creatures could drain them. She was fighting an attrition war against entropy itself, growing life faster than it could be consumed. Tidus held the south — the shell shovel's coral reinforcing the crumbling shoreline while his Haki-compressed snow cemented each growth into place, his engineering keeping ground under their feet that the creatures were trying to dissolve. His Conqueror's Haki — domain-level, sovereign — pressed outward in steady waves, and the creatures that entered his radius slowed. Not stopped. Slowed. His will was not enough to dominate them, but it was enough to make them hesitate, and hesitation was enough for Kawamatsu's Ryou-infused blade to find the gaps in their flickering bodies.

Claude held the centre. Not as a fighter — as a conductor. The Mote network, augmented by Perona's ghost surveillance, provided total battlefield awareness. Every creature, every ally, every shifting piece of terrain — mapped, tracked, and communicated through the network in real-time. Claude routed intelligence to all six parties simultaneously: targeting data to Mihawk, gravitational anchor points to Fujitora, drain-rate calculations to Smoothie, terrain stability readings to Kawamatsu, creature phase-shift patterns to Perona's ghost scouts, atmospheric composition data to Rime. Six factions that had never trained together, fighting as a coordinated force because a ship's spirit refused to let them fight as six separate disasters.

The Homies deployed by family. Maru's Roundlings formed rolling defensive perimeters around the wounded. Hinoko's Embers provided fire support — Kagari's living lantern glow the only steady light source when the Chemist Class was redeploying. Shizuka's Dustlings ran intelligence beneath the canopy, invisible, silent, mapping creature tunnel networks that the ghosts could not reach. Zenmai's Gearlings kept the Meridian's engines alive under atmospheric conditions that were corroding every mechanical system aboard. Tsuyu's Sproutlings healed — constantly, desperately, their tiny botanical bodies working overtime to mend injuries that the creatures' draining touch made resistant to normal recovery.

The Copplings fought as a mechanical combat unit — Rivet and Gauge on the front line, Anvil charging upside-down across the hull, Shuttle running ammunition at speeds the creatures could not track, Patch treating wounded between salvos. The Vanguard — the Twelve Who Stand — deployed across the perimeter in pairs, their Elite Officer-class capabilities carving through organisms that outmassed them by a factor of a thousand. Small, adorable, and absolutely devastating.

And the Guiding Stars broadcasted from the Constellation Room. Forty-six toys whose Conqueror's Haki exceeded Marine measurement equipment, their collective will pressing outward through the hull and across the island in waves that made even Mihawk's hawk eyes narrow. The creatures faltered in those waves — not defeated, but noticed. For the briefest instant, the Stars' will forced the rift-creatures to acknowledge that they were being observed by something that refused to look away.
Convergence Protocols — One-Time Combination Attacks

As at Elbaf, these attacks were never planned. They emerged from the pressure of six factions forced into the same shrinking space, finding synchronicities that no amount of training could have produced. Claude recorded them not as doctrine but as evidence — proof that when the world demands the impossible, strangers can become a single weapon.
Rime + Fujitora — "Falling Star Furnace"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Fujitora raised his cane-sword and pulled. A meteor — torn from the upper atmosphere by the Zushi Zushi no Mi's gravitational command — descended toward the island like the fist of a god that had finally lost patience. It should have destroyed everything. It would have destroyed everything, if a small chinchilla made of superheated plasma had not launched himself directly into its path.

Rime, in full Logia dissolution, wrapped himself around the falling meteor like a second atmosphere. His thermal output — already star-surface hot in combat — amplified by the friction of re-entry into something that the Mote network's sensors simply labelled EXCESSIVE. The meteor hit the battlefield not as stone but as a falling sun — white-hot, plasma-wrapped, trailing a corona of Rime's vapour that turned the air itself into a weapon. Every creature in the impact zone was simultaneously crushed by Fujitora's gravitational well and vaporised by Rime's heat. The crater steamed for three days afterward.

The blind admiral and the small chinchilla. No words exchanged. No plan discussed. Fujitora felt Rime's thermal signature through his Observation Haki and understood what the chinchilla intended to do the way a musician understands a chord change — instinctively, structurally, as a consequence of listening well. Rime felt the meteor's gravitational pull through his Logia body and understood the trajectory the way fire understands updraft — as an invitation. They communicated through physics. Through the shared language of force and heat and falling.

Rime, afterward, called it "the second-best day of my life." When asked what the first was, he said: "Elbaf. Obviously. I rode a giant squirrel through the void. You can't top that." Fujitora, characteristically, bowed his head toward the sound of Rime's voice and said: "Your crew raises remarkable companions, Swarm Sovereign. That chinchilla fights like he has nothing to lose and everything to protect. I find that admirable."
Tidus + Kawamatsu — "The Current and the Kappa"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Two beings of the water. A polar bear-otter with a coral-growing weapon and an engineer's understanding of force. A pufferfish fish-man with a sumo wrestler's intuition for leverage and a samurai's precision with the blade. They found each other on the crumbling southern shoreline where the island was dissolving into the sea, and they held the line.

Kawamatsu's Kappa-ryu creates focused shockwaves — sumo technique channelled through a blade, each strike generating concussive force that travels through water and earth in directed bursts. Raw. Powerful. Imprecise. Tidus drove his shell shovel into the earth and coral erupted along Kawamatsu's attack vectors — living funnels, resonance chambers, amplification channels — that shaped the shockwaves. Where the coral needed reinforcement, Tidus packed his Snow-Snow Fruit ice into the joints; where the ground crumbled, his bio-film sealed the fractures. The samurai's raw power, guided through the shipwright's structural precision, became something neither could produce alone: surgical devastation. Shockwaves that could target individual creatures amid a horde. Blasts that curved around allies. Concussive force that arrived from impossible angles because Tidus' coral-and-ice channels redirected it like water through pipes.

They worked in silence. The bear-otter building, the kappa striking. Tidus would plant the shovel and coral would bloom from its blade, and Kawamatsu would immediately understand the structure's purpose — strike here, at this angle, with this force — as if the coral itself was a sentence he could read. Two artisans of the water, speaking the tidal language of pressure and flow. At one point Tidus' enormous paws clapped together — the deep resonant POP of his Paw-Clap Shockwave — and the concussive burst cleared a path that Kawamatsu filled with a perfect overhead strike before the debris had settled.

Tidus said afterward: "He hits like the ocean when it remembers it's angry. I just gave the anger a direction." Kawamatsu bowed — the full samurai bow, forehead to the ground — and said: "In Wano, the great engineers are honoured equally with the great warriors. Today I understand why."
Petal + Mihawk — "The Garden of Black Thorns"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

The world's greatest blade. The world's gentlest gardener. It should not have worked. It worked beautifully.

Petal deployed Briarcrest — the botanical parasaurolophus — and sounded the war-horn crest. The canopy erupted. Thorns, vines, flowering tendrils, root systems — a labyrinth of living plant matter that spread across the eastern perimeter faster than the creatures could drain it. Petal was growing faster than entropy, her Dryad Form's botanical output exceeding the rift-creatures' draining capacity for the first time in the battle. The eastern flank stabilised. And then — without coordination, without a word, without even looking at her — Mihawk sent Yoru's flying slashes into the maze.

The slashes did not travel straight through. They followed the root system — guided by the roots' own architecture, bending around trunks, threading through thorn corridors, arriving at targets from angles that should have been geometrically impossible. Yoru's cutting force, directed by Petal's living labyrinth, became an invisible blade network. The creatures were cut apart by a sword they could not see, along paths chosen by roots they could not escape. The garden was a firing solution. Every branch was a trajectory. Every thorn was a secondary projectile, and the world's strongest swordsman was the ammunition.

Mihawk said one word afterward: "Elegant." For a man who speaks in sentences shorter than his blade, this was the equivalent of a standing ovation. Petal blushed beneath her feathers and whispered that the garden chose the paths, not she. The blade chose to follow, not she. She was, as always, deflecting credit to the living things around her. Mihawk, uncharacteristically, seemed to approve. His hawk eyes lingered on the chick — on the tiny, gentle botanist who had just turned an entire forest into a delivery system for the deadliest blade in the world — and something in his expression shifted. Not softened. Acknowledged.
Perona + The Meridian — "The Haunted Fleet"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Perona's ghosts merged with the Meridian's entire extended family. The result was unprecedented, chaotic, and — in Perona's own words, delivered through happy tears — "the most adorable apocalypse I have ever caused."

Negative Hollows rode inside the Roundlings, turning Maru's gentle family into carriers of existential dread. When a Roundling bumped into a creature — and Roundlings are always bumping into things — the hollow inside detonated on contact. The rift-creatures were immune to depression; they had no selfhood to depress. But the sheer volume of negative energy, amplified by the proximity of forty-six Guiding Stars broadcasting Conqueror's Haki, created a physical pressure wave that stunned them instead. Weaponised sadness, turned kinetic by the will of toys that exceed Marine measurement equipment.

Explosive Mini Hollows were loaded into the Copplings as warheads — Rivet packed them into his tools, Anvil charged them across the hull on her magnetic claws, Shuttle delivered them at speeds the ghosts could never achieve alone, Bobbin wrapped them in cloth to muffle the approach. The Vanguard — the Twelve Who Stand — moved through Perona's ghost surveillance network with perfect battlefield awareness, their Elite Officer-class capabilities directed by spectral intelligence they had never possessed before. Harui stitched ghost-trails into living tripwires. Gankaku used hollows as cushioned impact enhancers. Hoshikuzu painted targeting markers on the creatures using ghost-light that only the Vanguard could see.

And the Guiding Stars — broadcasting from the Constellation Room — resonated through the ghost network, their unassessable Haki flowing through Perona's hollows like a signal through a relay tower. The Ghost Princess had never had an antenna this powerful. The entire battlefield was saturated in a unique frequency: the Stars' sovereign will, carried by ghosts, into every corner of the fight.

Perona was crying. Not from sadness. From joy. She had never seen an army this adorable. She had never felt this useful. The Ghost Princess — surrounded by plushies and copplings and little homies that bumped into her knees and looked up at her with button eyes — was having the time of her life. "Horo-horo-horo-horo!" rang across the battlefield, and for once, the laugh was entirely genuine.
Ajay + Smoothie — "Golden Squeeze"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Smoothie's Shibo Shibo no Mi wrings liquid from anything — living or inanimate. When she applied this to the rift-creatures, the result was horrifying and effective in equal measure. The creatures' inter-dimensional fluid — a substance that existed partially in this reality and partially in whatever lay beyond the rift — was extracted in gushing streams of bioluminescent liquid that Smoothie absorbed into her own body. She grew. And grew. The Sweet Commander expanded to the size of a giant, then beyond, her body swelling with alien vitality that made her claymore strikes hit with the weight of a collapsing building.

Behind her, Ajay's swarm flooded the desiccated husks. Workers colonised the empty fur shells — the creatures' ancient armour, now drained of the bodies that had worn it, repurposed as mobile fortresses for the swarm. The Herculean Class wore fur plate like siege armour. The Soldier Class burrowed into the creatures' bioelectric nervous systems and rode them — puppeteering the dead husks into the living horde, turning the creatures' own bodies into weapons against their kin.

One takes apart. The other repurposes. One empties. The other fills. The rhythm was brutal, efficient, and eerily complementary — as if the Shibo Shibo no Mi and the Mure Mure no Mi had been designed to work in sequence. Smoothie drained the world of life; Ajay's swarm immediately occupied the space left behind. Nature, it seems, truly does abhor a vacuum.

Smoothie noted, with characteristic deadpan, that the creatures' fluid "had an interesting bouquet. Vintage, certainly. Pre-geological, possibly. I would not recommend it as a beverage." Ajay, grinning inside a suit of ancient fur armour, replied: "I'm wearing something older than the planet and it fits perfectly. Is that cool or terrifying?" Smoothie considered this. "Both," she said. "Which is the only honest answer."
All Six — "The Sextet Convergence"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — ALL PARTIES

Hour twelve. The island had lost two-thirds of its mass. The rift was widening. The creature output was accelerating. Six parties, exhausted, bloodied, running on will alone. And Claude, from the centre of the Mote network, made a calculation and broadcast it to all channels simultaneously: "The rift closes if we destroy the anchor organisms on either side. Thirty seconds of unified force at the rift's base. All assets. Everything. Now."

Fujitora pinned the largest anchor organisms with gravitational fields so dense that light bent around them — the blind admiral's gravity pressing Pre-Index behemoths flat against the crumbling earth. Mihawk's Yoru carved the corridor — flying slashes of such precision that they threaded between allies, between swarm castes, between Roundlings and ghosts, cutting a clean path to the rift's base without touching a single friendly. Smoothie, massive from absorbed fluids, swept the flanks — her enormous claymore clearing approach lanes with each swing. Perona's ghost network provided total battlefield awareness, her astral form hovering above the convergence point and calling positions with the cold clarity of someone whose body was safely hidden three hundred meters away. Kawamatsu's Kappa-ryu shockwaves cleared the final approach — Ryou-infused strikes that passed through the creatures' phase-shifting armour and struck what was underneath.

And the Meridian. Full deployment. Every caste, every family, every Coppling, every Star. Claude conducting. Rime detonating. Petal growing. Tidus building. The Vanguard on the hull. The Homies in the canopy. The Harvest Swarm guarding the retreat vector. Akebono dividing into hundreds of copies to cushion the blowback. Xylem keeping the engines alive.

For twelve seconds — Claude timed it precisely — the island went silent. Not because the creatures stopped. Because every creature within the convergence zone was simultaneously pinned, cut, drained, haunted, crushed, grown over, phase-locked, burned, or occupied. Twelve seconds of absolute dominance by six parties that had never trained together, never planned together, and never intended to be in the same place at the same time.

The anchor organisms died. The rift contracted — not closed, but tightened, its edges drawing together like a wound remembering how to heal. The creature output dropped to a trickle. The island stopped dying.

Nobody celebrated. Nobody spoke of those twelve seconds for a very long time — because acknowledging them would mean acknowledging that a pirate, a Marine Admiral, a Sweet Commander, a Ghost Princess, a Wano samurai, and the Sovereign Swarm Architect could, if forced, fight as one. And the implications of that — for the world's power structures, for the concept of faction, for the very idea that these people were supposed to be enemies — were almost as terrifying as the creatures themselves.
