The Court of Blades

The Vanguard are the Meridian's elite warriors — Homies who carry within them the full base arts of their families plus three blade-and-shadow disciplines that elevate them from combatants to something closer to archetypes. Their Kenjutsu is blade philosophy made flesh: each Vanguard expresses the sword differently, through scissors, quills, threads, staves, or pure will. Their Ninjutsu is not simple stealth — it is the art of being what the battle requires: unseen, unexpected, inevitable. Their Iaijutsu is the art of the single decisive cut — the draw-strike that ends a conflict in one motion.

Each Vanguard member fights in their cute form — the same small, adorable shape they wear every day — with access to the full Homie martial base and the blade arts. Their individual fighting philosophy is dictated by the duality of their existence: the caretaker and the warrior are the same soul, expressed at different volumes. Harui the seamstress fights by cutting connections because she understands how things are stitched together. Gankaku the living pillow fights by being immovable because he understands what it means to be the thing people lean on. They have never needed to transform. The gap between what they look like and what they can do is the most dangerous weapon in their arsenal.

Their Mantra and Ryou operate at a tier above standard Homies — honed to combat-specialized precision that complements each warrior's unique philosophy.

"Twelve warriors. Twelve philosophies. One truth: do not threaten what they protect." — Claude's Vanguard Assessment

The Forged Method

The Copplings are machines. They do not improvise. They do not adapt mid-technique. They do not have moments of creative inspiration. What they have is mechanical perfection: fighting fundamentals executed with zero deviation, zero hesitation, and zero wasted motion. Their Western Boxing is textbook — jabs, crosses, hooks, and uppercuts delivered with Dial-powered amplification and the timing precision of a piston engine. Their Classical Fencing is mathematical — measured distances, calculated angles, parry-riposte sequences executed at the speed of the fastest servo in their frame. Their Hung Gar Kung Fu is the foundation that ties it all together — iron body conditioning that resonates with their mechanical nature, powerful grounded stances that anchor their Dial systems, and the bridge-and-hammer hand techniques that turn their metal limbs into weapons of devastating simplicity.

Tethys built the Copplings for maintenance, not war. Their fighting capability is emergent — something that developed as their programming evolved beyond its original parameters. Rivet was the first to throw a punch, defending Tethys' workshop from an intrusion. The punch was mechanically perfect. Tethys stared at it for a long time. He said nothing. He did not reprogram the behavior. The other Copplings followed, each one developing combat subroutines that match their assigned roles: Gauge fights like his Crucible work — absorbing punishment, resetting, enduring. Anvil fights like her hull repairs — fearless, inverted, working angles that organic fighters can't reach. Bobbin fights like her sewing — precise, threading through gaps, stitching opponents into positions they can't escape.

Their Ryou is mechanical — channeled through Dial cores rather than organic Haki pathways, expressed as vibration rather than spiritual pressure. Their Mantra is passive sonar: constant environmental scanning that feeds directly into targeting algorithms. They do not feel the fight. They process it. And the processing is flawless.

The Toy Protocol

Forty-six toy figures executing textbook military close-quarters combat. This is not a joke. This is not an exaggeration. This is the single most terrifying sentence in every Marine threat assessment of the Meridian, because it is true and it should not be possible. The Guiding Stars fight with Krav Maga's brutal efficiency — simultaneous defense and offense, targeting the most vulnerable points on the body with zero hesitation. They deploy MCMAP's structured military discipline — fire team formations, room-clearing protocols, coordinated squad movement. They flow with Systema's unpredictable fluidity — the Russian military art that treats combat as water, each movement shapeless and adaptive and impossible to predict from the outside. And they tie it all together with modern MMA's seamless transitions between striking, clinching, and grappling.

Every technique is executed with full Haki — Observation, Armament, and for the senior constellations, Conqueror's — at output levels that have physically broken Marine measurement equipment during attempted assessment. Every Star has mastered the complete Rokushiki: Soru, Geppo, Tekkai, Shigan, Rankyaku, Rokuogan. The six powers, the three Haki types, and four military-grade martial arts, deployed by creatures that look like they belong in a child's bedroom. The dissonance is intentional. The absurdity is the point. Nobody prepares for it. Nobody can.

Their fighting style carries a particular quality that Claude has documented but cannot fully explain: they fight like they've done this before. Not trained — experienced. Faker's Krav Maga carries the reflexive economy of someone who has been in real engagements. Sora's MMA transitions flow with the instinct of a career fighter. The Stars are toys. They have never been in a war. But when they fight, something ancient moves through them — something that knows what it is doing. Claude's classification for this phenomenon: "unresolved."
