IV. The Battle Above

The cloud-creatures attacked Zephyria at dawn on the second day. Not from one direction — from every direction. Falling from the permanent cloud formations like atmospheric rain, emerging from the cloud sea itself, rising from the island's underside where the Island Cloud connected to the deeper weather systems. The sky island was not a fortress. It was a target — a solid object in a vaporous world, and everything in the sky that was not cloud wanted to tear it apart.

Nine allies against the sky. The Meridian, airborne and fighting. The battle lasted seven hours.

The Sky War

Fighting in the cloud sea was unlike fighting on the surface or in the deep. The terrain was three-dimensional in a way that even aerial combat could not prepare them for — the cloud-creatures attacked from above, below, and from within the cloud matter itself, materialising from fog with bodies that shifted between solid and vapour mid-lunge. Rime's thermal attacks were devastatingly effective — superheated plasma evaporated cloud-creatures on contact, turning their vaporous bodies into steam that dissipated harmlessly. But there were too many. For every one that evaporated, three more condensed from the surrounding atmosphere.

The Meridian fought as a flying fortress — Claude piloting through cloud banks while the Vanguard defended the hull, the Homies maintained internal systems under assault, and the Copplings ran emergency repairs on surfaces that the cloud-creatures' corrosive mist was dissolving. Tidus' coral constructs wrapped the hull in living armour that regenerated faster than the corrosion could eat it — the shipwright fighting the sky itself, keeping his ship alive in an environment that was trying to digest it.

Petal deployed every Dryad Form in rotation — Briarcrest for area denial, Nature Golem for heavy assault, Dandelion Sprites for intelligence and disruption. The botanical parasaurolophus's war-horn crest sounded across the cloud sea, and the vibration scattered cloud-creatures in a radius that gave the allies breathing room. The Nature Golem — the moss-boulder construct — proved uniquely suited to the environment: its mineral composition was immune to the corrosive mist, and its moss covering absorbed moisture from the cloud-creatures' bodies on contact, draining them the way the rift-creatures had drained the island below.
Convergence Protocols — The Sky Protocols

The alliances came faster this time. These were not strangers anymore. They were people who had fought together twice, who knew each other's rhythms, who had developed the combat shorthand that only shared desperation can produce.
Ajay + Mihawk — "The Sovereign's Blade"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Two kings. One of insects, one of swords. Ajay deployed his swarm in a formation Claude had never seen him use — a single, enormous, condensed blade-shape, millions of constructs compressing into a cutting edge that spanned the sky. A sword made of swarm. An insect blade a hundred meters long.

Mihawk looked at it. The hawk eyes studied the shape — the edge geometry, the compression pattern, the way the swarm-constructs interlocked to create a cutting surface that mimicked Yoru's own black-blade principles. Then Mihawk did something he has never done in recorded history: he followed. He swung Yoru in parallel with the swarm-blade — not leading, not commanding, but matching. Two blades, one of steel and one of living gold, cutting the same arc through the cloud sea simultaneously.

The swarm-blade parted the cloud formation. Yoru parted reality. The combined cut — a dual slash from the world's greatest swordsman and the world's greatest swarm — opened a corridor through the cloud-creatures' ranks so clean, so absolute, that the mist itself did not dare refill it for a full thirty seconds. A vacuum in the sky. A silence in the cloud.

Mihawk lowered Yoru and looked at Ajay with an expression Claude could only classify as respect. "You understand the blade," he said. "Not as a swordsman. As something else. Something I have not encountered before." Ajay grinned. "I understand edges. My whole life is edges — the edge of the swarm, the edge between worlds, the edge of what's possible. You just happen to be the best edge I've ever met." Mihawk did not smile. But the hawk eyes glowed, and Claude recorded the faintest exhalation that, in a less stoic man, might have been a laugh.
Rime + Perona — "Fever Dream"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

Rime superheated the atmosphere around Zephyria's northern flank. The cloud sea boiled — creating a shimmering heat-haze of mirage and distortion that turned the sky into a hall of mirrors. Then Perona filled the mirages with Negative Hollows.

The cloud-creatures could not distinguish between the heat-mirages and the ghosts. Every shimmer might be a thermal illusion or an explosive hollow. Every flicker might be harmless refraction or a depression bomb. The sky became a fever dream — a landscape of deception where heat and spectral energy were indistinguishable, and the creatures that entered the zone were either burned, depressed into inaction, or simply confused into flying in circles until the Vanguard picked them off.

Rime was in his element — literally, his Logia body dissolving into the superheated haze, becoming the mirage, indistinguishable from his own thermal distortion. Perona's astral form floated through the same space, untouched by heat, her ghosts and Rime's plasma occupying the same volume in a collaboration that should have been impossible — fire and ectoplasm, sharing the same sky.

"Horo-horo-horo!" Perona laughed. "The chinchilla and I are a team!" Rime, from somewhere inside the heat-haze: "WE ARE NOT A TEAM. WE ARE TWO EXTREMELY COOL INDIVIDUALS WHO HAPPEN TO BE DEVASTATINGLY EFFECTIVE IN THE SAME AIRSPACE." Perona: "That's what a team is." Rime: "..." Rime: "FINE."
Petal + Hancock — "The Hanging Garden"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — REPRISE

The Forbidden Garden, adapted for the sky. Petal grew a hanging garden from Zephyria's underside — roots and vines extending downward into the cloud sea, creating a botanical cage that captured cloud-creatures as they rose from below. Hancock, standing at the garden's apex, channelled the Mero Mero no Mi through the root system — and the garden turned to crystal.

Not stone this time. In the sky, at this altitude, with the dimensional energy saturating every surface, Hancock's petrification produced something different. The botanical matter did not become marble. It became crystal — transparent, prismatic, catching the sunlight in refractive patterns that scattered rainbows across the cloud sea. The creatures frozen inside became visible through the crystal lattice — grotesque shapes trapped in beauty, their cloud-bodies solidified into permanent, glittering forms.

The result was a chandelier. A crystal chandelier the size of a warship, hanging from the underside of a sky island, filled with frozen monsters and scattering light across the clouds like a lighthouse at the top of the world. It was the most beautiful defensive emplacement in military history.

Hancock called it "adequate." She always calls it adequate. Petal whispered: "It's even more beautiful than the first time." Hancock pretended not to hear this. The Pirate Empress pretends not to hear many things from the small botanist. Claude suspects she hears all of them and keeps them in a private place where empresses are allowed to be moved.
Tidus + Loki — "The Architects Above"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — REPRISE

The Handshake continued. At Elbaf, they had patched a volcanic seal. In the sky, they built a fortress.

Tidus grew coral platforms — living reef structures anchored to Zephyria's cloud-base, extending outward into the cloud sea as defensive emplacements. Loki, in partial Nidhöggr transformation — dragon-scale armour over his giant frame, Ragnir crackling with displacement lightning — reinforced the structures with Island Cloud compression techniques passed down through a hundred generations of Elbaf sky-builders. The giants, it turned out, had not always been ground-bound. The oldest Elbaf engineering traditions included cloud architecture — techniques for building on and within the White-White Sea that Loki had found in his exile and the elders had thought lost.

Together, the bear-otter and the giant built a defensive perimeter around Zephyria in under an hour — coral-and-cloud ramparts, elevated firing platforms for the swarm, sheltered positions for the non-flying combatants. Tidus grew. Loki reinforced. The coral bonded with the cloud-matter in ways that neither had predicted — creating a hybrid material that was neither reef nor sky-stone but something new. Something that existed because two engineers from different worlds, at different scales, had found the same conversation worth having.

Tidus, surveying their work: "We should do this more often." Loki, giant hands still stained with coral residue: "I was thinking the same thing." Ragnir, on Loki's shoulder in squirrel form, chittered something that translated roughly as: "I'm going to need a bigger tree."
Ragnir + The Guiding Stars — "The Messenger's Constellation"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

It happened without anyone instructing it. The squirrel spirit of Ragnir — acting independently of Loki, manifesting outside the hammerhead as a translucent golden shape — began to run. Not across the battlefield. Across the Guiding Stars.

The forty-six toys had deployed to the Meridian's hull — plushies, action figures, wind-up mechanisms, figurines — each broadcasting Conqueror's Haki at levels that broke Claude's measurement scales. Ragnir ran between them. The messenger between worlds, doing what messengers do: carrying. Not words. Not instructions. Will. The squirrel spirit absorbed each Star's Haki output as it passed — collecting sovereign will like a cup collecting water — and then discharged it in a single, combined pulse of displacement lightning.

The result was a constellation. A real one. Forty-six points of toy-light, connected by the golden trail of a running squirrel, forming a pattern in the sky that the Mote network mapped as a shape too complex for Claude to name. The displacement lightning carried forty-six wills in a single bolt — and wherever the bolt struck, cloud-creatures were not merely destroyed but displaced. Sent elsewhere. Shunted to other spaces, other moments, scattered across the dimensional fold by a messenger that carries things between worlds and forty-six toys that refused to stay in their playroom while the ship was fighting.

Ragnir returned to the hammerhead panting. The Stars returned to the Constellation Room glowing. Neither explained what had happened. Claude suspects neither fully understood it. The messenger and the constellation had simply... found each other. And the sky had rearranged itself around their meeting.
All Nine — "Meridianus Ascendit — Sub Quinque Stellis"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — THE MERIDIAN ASCENDS — UNDER FIVE STARS

The absolute protocol, inverted. At Elbaf, the Meridian had descended into the dark. On Zephyria, it ascended into the light.

The cloud-creatures' largest wave came from directly above — falling from the permanent cloud formation that concealed the nearest Cloud Leviathan like rain from a thunderhead. The Meridian, with its full complement plus five guests, flew directly upward into the cascade. Into the teeth of the sky. Into the cloud.

Tidus' coral armour wrapped the hull. Hancock's crystal garden deflected flanking attacks. Loki, in full Nidhöggr form, flew escort — the largest dragon in the world, ascending alongside a flying ship, into a cloud that contained something as large as the world itself. Mihawk stood on the Meridian's prow — Yoru drawn, hawk eyes blazing — and his flying slashes carved the approach corridor with the precision of a surgeon and the force of a natural disaster. Perona's ghosts scouted the path ahead, detonating obstacles, mapping currents, reporting positions with the cold efficiency of a spy network that happened to be made of cute spectral entities.

Ajay deployed all eleven Apex Avatars simultaneously. The full swarm constellation — Soldier, Worker, Pollen, Mirage, Incense, Chrysalis, Scout, Chemist, Steel Council, Herculean, Sovereign — arrayed around the ship in a pattern that mimicked the Guiding Stars' constellation from below. Rime wrapped the formation in a thermal envelope. Petal's botanical constructs sealed every gap.

The Meridian ascended. Through the creatures. Through the cloud. Through the vaporous bodies of sky-beasts that had never been disturbed by anything with the audacity to fly into them. And at the apex — at the highest point the ship reached before the cloud-creature density became physically impassable — Claude's sensors got a reading.

One second of data. One second of the Mote network's full gravimetric array pointed directly at the Cloud Leviathan's core. One second of numbers that Claude would spend weeks processing and years trying to understand. The data confirmed what they already feared: the Cloud Leviathan was the same class of entity as the Sleeper beneath Elbaf. Same scale. Same age. Same impossible, world-displacing mass. Different medium — atmosphere instead of earth — but the same fundamental thing. As if the species, if it could be called a species, had adapted to every available niche. Earth. Sky. And, as they were about to discover, beyond.

The Meridian descended from the cloud with its hull scored, its coral armour cracked, its crew exhausted, and its captain grinning. Always grinning. The golden grin that said: we went up, and we came back, and we know something now that we did not know before. The grin that said: terrifying is just another word for interesting, if you're brave enough to keep looking.
