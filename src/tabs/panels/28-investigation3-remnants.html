II. The Remnants

The ruins on Zephyria were not one civilization. They were many.

Layer upon layer, built and rebuilt and abandoned and rebuilt again, spanning what the Archivist estimated as at least three hundred years of intermittent occupation. Different construction techniques. Different material preferences. Different architectural philosophies. The only constant: every builder had used materials and methods that did not belong to this world. The ruins were a geological record of dimensional refugees — a stratigraphy of strangers, each layer representing a different group of arrivals who had found this sky island, built shelter, lived for a time, and then vanished.

Three Scripts, Three Worlds

The ruins bore inscriptions in three distinct writing systems. The first was the same alphabet Ajay had identified on the rift island — English, the language of his world. The second was a script that no one in the party could identify — angular, geometric, written in vertical columns, with a mathematical precision that suggested a culture for whom language and calculation were the same discipline. The third was something that made Claude's Archivist hesitate: a flowing, organic script that bore structural similarities to Poneglyph carving — the same deep-grooved letterforms, the same resistance to weathering — but in a completely different language. As if someone from a world that had its own Poneglyphs had carved messages using the same technique but different words.

Three writing systems. Three worlds. Three separate dimensional intrusions, each depositing people from different realities into the same sky island. The implications were staggering: the rifts did not all connect to Ajay's world. They connected to multiple worlds. Multiple realities, multiple dimensions, each producing its own refugees when the Sleepers stirred and the walls between worlds buckled.
The Garden

Petal found it first. In the deepest layer of the ruins — the oldest construction, buried beneath centuries of subsequent building — a garden had survived. Not a wild garden. A cultivated garden. Someone had planted it deliberately, with care, with knowledge of soil composition and sunlight angles and seasonal growth patterns. And the plants in it were from at least four different worlds.

Sky-island vegetation grew beside specimens that Petal's Dryad senses classified as impossible — plants whose cellular structure operated on photosynthetic pathways that did not exist in this world's biology. A tree whose bark was slightly warm to the touch, as if its metabolism ran hotter than any terrestrial species. Flowers that opened and closed in response to the dimensional frequency rather than light. Moss that grew in fractal patterns of such mathematical precision that Tidus spent twenty minutes measuring it and declared it "the most beautiful engineering I have seen that was not built by hands."

Someone — from some world, in some century — had arrived on this sky island alone and frightened and had done what gardeners do: they had planted things. And the things they planted had survived them. Had outlived whatever happened to them. Had grown and cross-pollinated with plants from other dimensions and other arrivals, creating a hybrid garden that existed nowhere else in any reality.

Petal sat in the center of the garden for a long time. She did not speak. She placed her hands on the soil and listened — not with her ears but with the Dryad awareness that connects her to every growing thing — and what she heard was a chorus. Hundreds of years of gardeners from dozens of worlds, each adding to the same plot of soil, each leaving behind the only thing a displaced person can truly give to a world that is not theirs: growth.
Evidence of the Recent

Not all the traces were ancient. Some were recent — within the last decade. Campfire remnants. Fishing line made from materials unavailable in this world. A crude shelter built against the inner wall of the oldest ruin, its construction suggesting someone who understood structural engineering but had never worked with cloud-matter before. And, scratched into the shelter's back wall with a sharp stone, a message:

"To anyone who comes after: The way back is not through the rift. The rift only opens from the other side. I have tried. I have tried for three years. Go down. Find the ocean. Find people. Survive. The world is strange but it is not empty. The two moons are not wrong. They are just different. — K."

Just "K." A single initial. A person who had spent three years on a sky island trying to force their way back through a one-way door, and who had finally accepted the truth and descended to the Blue Sea below. Alive, presumably. Out there, somewhere. Another seed scattered by cosmic wind, planted in impossible soil.

Ajay read the message three times. Then he added a response, carved beneath it with a blade borrowed from Mihawk — who offered it without being asked, in the closest thing to gentleness the World's Strongest Swordsman has ever demonstrated:

"K — I came the same way. It gets easier. It doesn't stop being strange, but it gets easier. If you can hear this, come to the sea. We're looking for you. — A."
Loki's Discovery

In the deepest chamber of the oldest ruin, Loki found something that Ragnir reacted to with violent urgency — the squirrel spirit in the hammerhead shrieking, pressing against the metal, its carved eyes blazing with the light of the World Tree's messenger. What Loki found was a carving — not a message, not an inscription, but a map.

A three-dimensional map, rendered in the stone of the chamber wall with a precision that exceeded any cartographic technique the Archivist had recorded. It depicted the world — not just the Blue Sea and the Grand Line, but the planet in cross-section. And scattered across the cross-section, marked with the same dimensional-frequency notation that the rift had produced, were points. Dozens of them. In the deepest trenches. In the highest peaks. In the clouds. In the bedrock. In locations that corresponded to no charted territory — blank spaces on every map, avoided by every navigation system, the places that cartography had agreed to pretend did not exist.

The map was not of the world. It was a map of them. The Sleepers. The dormant entities. Plotted by someone — from some dimension, in some century — who had arrived on this sky island and spent their remaining years mapping the locations of every sleeping titan on the planet.

Claude's Mote network overlaid the carving's coordinates against its own unconscious data — the trace frequencies that had been accumulating since Elbaf — and the match was exact. The ancient map and Claude's involuntary cartography agreed: there were over a hundred of them. On the planet alone.
