The Gentle Tide

The Roundlings fight exactly the way they live: with unyielding softness. Their martial expression draws overwhelmingly from Judo and Hapkido — arts built on the principle that yielding to force is the most efficient way to defeat it. Every Roundling, from Maru to little Lull, carries the same core philosophy: you cannot hurt what refuses to resist. Their spherical bodies are not a liability — they are the architecture. A Roundling absorbs an incoming strike by rolling with it, converts the kinetic energy into rotational momentum, and redirects it as a throw, a sweep, or a devastating counter-roll that carries Ryou through every point of contact.

Their Taekkyon influence manifests as a deceptive, dance-like footwork pattern — the rolling waddle that makes Roundlings look harmless is actually Taekkyon's flowing step adapted for round bodies. Each waddle shifts weight, each sway sets up an angle for a Judo throw. Their Vale Tudo is the surprise: when true danger threatens the family, the cuddly exterior drops and the Roundlings fight with zero rules, zero restraint, and a Tethys-Resonant Ryou that hardens their bodies into bouncing wrecking balls. Maru's Mantra is domestic-grade awareness refined to combat sensitivity — she knows where every family member is, where every threat vector exists, and where every cup of tea is getting cold, all simultaneously.

"They look like they should be in a nursery. They fight like the nursery fights back." — Marine Threat Assessment, Post-Meridian Encounter

The Garden's Patience

The Sproutlings do not fight like warriors. They fight like plants: slowly, inevitably, and with a patience that outlasts every storm. Their martial expression is dominated by Hapkido's soft circular philosophy and Taekkyon's flowing, dance-like movement — both filtered through Petal's botanical resonance into something that feels less like combat and more like gardening. An aggressive gardening where the weeds are enemy combatants and the Sproutlings have decided they need to be composted.

Their Mantra is the most unusual among the Homie families — they don't read combat intent so much as biological state. A Sproutling can sense stress hormones, elevated heart rate, muscle tension, and the tiny shifts in body chemistry that precede action. Tsuyu cries when she senses pain in others, but her tears are not weakness — they are dewdrops of Ryou, and everything they touch becomes a conduit for the Sproutlings' patient, inexorable control. Their Judo manifests as root-like ground control: once a Sproutling establishes a grip, the hold grows tighter with time, like a vine claiming a trellis. Their Vale Tudo exists only in theory — Tsuyu has never authorized it. The Sproutlings have never needed it. Nothing outruns a patient garden.

"They didn't attack us. They just... grew around us. And then we couldn't move. And then it was over." — Unnamed Marine Officer

Their Taekkyon manifests as wild, rapid-fire kicks thrown from impossible angles — less "flowing dance" and more "small fire tornado with feet." Their Judo exists primarily as a delivery mechanism: grab the opponent, cling to them, and raise core temperature until the opponent is forced to throw themselves just to escape the heat. Their Rime-Resonant Ryou is expressed as thermal amplification — every strike carries heat that burns from the inside, every touch leaves a thermal signature, and prolonged contact results in internal combustion.

Their Mantra is combat-tuned and chaotic — they read attack trajectories the way sparks read wind currents: instinctively, reactively, and with a gleeful disregard for what the information means beyond "dodge or don't, either way it'll be fun."

The Precision Engine

The Gearlings fight with mechanical perfection. Where other Homie families express the four base arts through emotion, instinct, or elemental affinity, the Gearlings express them through engineering. Every technique is calculated. Every strike targets a structural weakness identified by Mantra-grade diagnostics. Every movement follows a precisely timed rhythm set by Zenmai's internal mainspring. The result is a fighting style that feels less like martial arts and more like being disassembled by very small, very polite mechanics.

Their Judo is leverage mathematics — angles and fulcrums calculated with microscopic precision. Their Hapkido is joint manipulation applied with the exactitude of a calibration wrench. Their Taekkyon rhythm is metronomic: every step, every sway, every kick lands exactly on the beat of Zenmai's tempo, creating a predictable-seeming pattern that is actually a diagnostic sequence — each beat tests a different structural weakness. Their Mantra reads mechanical systems like blueprints, identifying stress fractures, fatigue points, and structural vulnerabilities that organic fighters miss entirely. Their Ryou is applied with zero waste — every erg of energy delivered precisely where the engineering analysis says it will do the most damage.

Their Vale Tudo — what Zenmai calls "Emergency Override" — is the one technique that terrifies even the other Homie families. When a Gearling removes its limiters, all calculation stops and raw output replaces precision. Zenmai has forbidden it except in life-threatening situations. Kaseki from the Embers thinks it's the coolest thing he's ever seen.
