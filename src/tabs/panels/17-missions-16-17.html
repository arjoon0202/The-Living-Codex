The Line Between Justice and Revolution
Operation: Dragon's Crucible ・ Egghead Archipelago — Vegapunk's Former Research Corridor, New World
Vegapunk.

The genius who wanted to feed the world and ended up arming the machine that starves it. The man who dreamed of limitless energy and handed the Mother Flame to the Five Elders — the weapon that erased Lulusia Kingdom from existence, eight million souls reduced to a crater in the ocean because an ancient throne decided they had asked too many questions. The scientist who saw Bartholomew Kuma — a man, a father, a revolutionary — and turned him into a Pacifista, peeling away his humanity layer by layer until nothing remained but a weapon that couldn't even remember its daughter's name. The architect of the Seraphim — children's bodies cloned from Warlords, grown in tubes, trained to kill, denied the concept of selfhood before they could learn the word. Good intentions. Naive heart. Brilliant mind. And a trail of suffering so vast it could fill the Grand Line twice over.

Ajay didn't care about the intentions. He cared about Kuma's empty eyes. He cared about the Seraphim who would never know they were children. He cared about Lulusia — a kingdom that existed for centuries and then didn't, because a genius built a weapon and trusted the wrong people not to use it. There is a line where naive becomes complicit, where "I didn't mean for this to happen" becomes the most dangerous sentence in the world, and Vegapunk had crossed it so many times the line was invisible behind him.

The Meridian descended on Vegapunk's former research corridor — a chain of islands in the New World still housing World Government-affiliated laboratories, automated weapons factories, and Pacifista production lines. The mission was not assassination. It was dismantlement. Every factory. Every production line. Every piece of infrastructure that turned genius into atrocity. Ajay came to tear the machinery apart and salt the ground it stood on.

The Revolutionary Army arrived within the hour.

Not to help. To stop him.

Monkey D. Dragon — the World's Most Wanted Criminal, Supreme Commander of the Revolutionary Army, father of Monkey D. Luffy, son of Garp the Fist — descended from the sky with a wind that bent trees flat and turned the ocean spray into horizontal rain. Behind him: Sabo, Chief of Staff, the Mera Mera no Mi burning in his fists like a second sun. Karasu, North Army Commander, his body dissolving into a flock of soot-black crows that blotted the sky. Lindbergh, South Army Commander, cat-Mink inventor with gadgets strapped to every surface of his body, jetpack screaming. Morley, West Army Commander, a giant who moved through solid earth like water. Belo Betty, East Army Commander, standing at the rear with her flag raised — and every Revolutionary soldier within earshot suddenly fighting with the strength and conviction of someone who believed, with absolute certainty, that they could not lose.

Dragon's message was simple: Vegapunk, for all his sins, is a friend of the revolution. His knowledge — his real knowledge, the Void Century research, the truths the World Government would kill to suppress — is too important to destroy. The laboratories Ajay was targeting contained not just weapons but archives. Research that could free the world. And Dragon would not allow a pirate's vendetta to destroy the revolution's most valuable intellectual asset.

Ajay's response was simpler: "Good intentions don't un-erase Lulusia."

The battle divided into four fronts.

Dragon versus Ajay. The world's most dangerous revolutionary against the Hive Codex. Dragon fought with a style that defied classification — wind-based attacks that seemed less like a Devil Fruit and more like the weather itself had chosen a side. His Conqueror's Haki was staggering — not the raw, crushing pressure of the God's Knights, but something older, more philosophical, a sovereign will built not on authority but on conviction. Every blast of wind carried intent. Every pressure wave was a question: are you certain enough to keep standing? Ajay's swarm shredded in the gale-force currents — entire castes torn apart, reassembled, torn apart again. The Archivist couldn't find a pattern because Dragon wasn't using patterns. He was fighting the way storms fight — instinctively, inevitably, without repetition. Ajay's Bodhi — the Mandala Eye mapping Dragon's intent, the Golden Pulse reading his conviction — saw something terrifying: Dragon believed in what he was doing with the same absolute certainty that Ajay did. Two sovereign wills, both right, both unwilling to bend.

Petal versus Karasu. Sky against sky. Karasu's Susu Susu no Mi transformed him into a living flock of soot-crows — a Paramecia so refined it behaved like a Logia, his body dispersing into soot so completely that striking him was like punching smoke. His crows attacked from every angle simultaneously, each one carrying enough force to shatter stone. Petal answered with altitude and patience. Her Observation Haki tracked every soot particle, mapping Karasu's real body within the flock — the dense core that his fruit always protected. Her Dryad Forms — the Briar Ankylos charging through the crow-clouds, the Nature Golem swatting them aside like gnats, the Dandelion Sprites dispersing into the flock to scatter his formation — turned the aerial battle into a war of attrition. Karasu was intangible. Petal was relentless. Her roots grew into the sky itself, threading through clouds and anchoring to updrafts, creating a three-dimensional web that limited where the crows could reform. She couldn't touch him — but she could contain him, and containment was enough.

Rime versus Sabo. Fire against fire. Sabo's Mera Mera no Mi — the flame Logia once wielded by Portgas D. Ace — roared to life with a heat that could melt Marine warships. Against anyone else, this would have been decisive. Against Rime — a creature whose entire existence was thermal manipulation — it was a conversation. Rime didn't fight Sabo's fire with cold. He fought it with control. He absorbed heat from one direction and redirected it from another. He created thermal vacuums that snuffed Sabo's flames at their source. He turned Sabo's own fire into Rime's fuel — pulling the heat into himself, converting it to plasma, and launching it back at a frequency Sabo's Logia couldn't passively absorb. Sabo adapted — Ryusoken strikes imbued with Haki that bypassed thermal resistance, Dragon Claw attacks aimed at Rime's core rather than his vapour field. The two of them traded blows at a speed that turned the ground beneath them to glass and then shattered the glass and then fused the shards into something new. It was beautiful. It was brutal. Both of them were grinning.

Tidus versus Lindbergh and Morley. Two against one — and Tidus held. Lindbergh's gadgets were a problem — the Cool Shooter fired projectiles that bypassed Tidus' water barriers by freezing on contact, turning his defenses into brittle ice. The Laser Shooter cut through coral constructs with surgical precision. But Tidus was an engineer fighting engineers, and he understood the logic of every weapon Lindbergh deployed. His heavy paws batted gadgets out of the air mid-flight. His four limbs gave him ground mobility that Lindbergh's jetpack couldn't match in close quarters. Meanwhile, Morley attacked from below — the Oshi Oshi no Mi letting the giant emerge from the earth with devastating uppercuts that shattered Tidus' barrier formations from underneath. Tidus answered by driving his shell shovel into the ground and extending a coral network through the soil — calcified countermines that disrupted Morley's tunnelling, forcing the giant to surface where Tidus' barriers were thickest. It was a three-dimensional chess match: Lindbergh from above, Morley from below, and Tidus in the middle, building and rebuilding his fortress faster than two of the revolution's most capable fighters could tear it down.

The Meridian versus the Revolutionary Army. Claude coordinated the wider battlefield — the homies, the Copplings, the Vanguard, the Harvest Swarm, all of them engaged against the Revolutionary Army's rank and file. But Belo Betty stood at the revolution's rear, her flag raised, the Kobu Kobu no Mi singing through every soldier within range. Her power was simple and devastating: she made people believe they could win, and that belief became physical. Revolutionary soldiers who should have been outmatched by the Vanguard were fighting at twice their natural ability — not through Haki augmentation but through sheer morale-driven adrenaline, every muscle and reflex amplified by the absolute conviction that their cause was just. The Vanguard fought beautifully — Harui's grace, Kagami's reflections, Gankaku's blade work — but every Revolutionary they pushed back stood up again, Betty's power coursing through them like a second heartbeat. Xylem coordinated from the Meridian's deck, feeding Claude logistics data as fast as her logbook could record it. The Copplings held infrastructure. The homies held lines. But the battle was not tipping.

The escalation came from above.

Dragon increased his output. The winds became a typhoon — not metaphorical, an actual, localized typhoon centred on the research corridor, with Ajay at its eye. The swarm was being pulled apart by wind shear. Bodhi gave Ajay clarity — the Mandala Eye showed him Dragon's conviction deepening, the storm feeding on the revolutionary's belief that protecting knowledge was worth any cost. Ajay's own conviction burned in response. The Black Crown Protocol stirred — not V1, something deeper. The Sovereign's will began to reach past the swarm, past the insects, probing the soul-tethers that connected him to his companions. V2 was waking up.

The air darkened. The gold of Bodhi began to corrupt — not disappearing, but shifting, the warm golden light bleeding to black at its edges, the Mandala Eye's fractal patterns twisting into something that looked less like insight and more like command. Ajay's Conqueror's Haki pulsed — and every crew member on the battlefield felt it. Not as pressure. As a tug. A pull on their autonomy. The beginning of the Totality.

Rime felt his body begin to move toward a stance he hadn't chosen. Petal's roots started growing in a direction she hadn't willed. Tidus' barriers began reshaping according to a design he didn't recognize. Claude felt the Mote network trying to reorganize itself around a single point of authority — not the distributed model Claude maintained, but a centralized hierarchy with Ajay at the apex and everything else as extension.

V2 was three heartbeats from full activation.

And then a fist fell from the sky.

Galaxy Impact.

Monkey D. Garp — "Garp the Fist," the Hero of the Marines, the man who cornered Gol D. Roger, the legend who refused every promotion because he couldn't be bothered with the paperwork of admiralty — had come to visit his son. He descended from a Marine vessel with the casual momentum of someone stepping off a porch, and his fist — Conqueror's Haki crackling in arcs so massive they spanned the entire island, Armament coating so dense the air itself turned black around his knuckles — came down between Dragon and Ajay like a judgement from the heavens.

The impact crater bisected the research corridor. The shockwave threw both combatants — and every fighter on every front — backward. Dragon's typhoon scattered. Ajay's emerging V2 shattered like glass struck by a church bell. The Mera Mera no Mi's flames guttered. Petal's roots snapped. Tidus' barriers imploded. Every Revolutionary soldier Betty had been boosting collapsed to their knees, not from the shockwave but from the Haki — Conqueror's at a frequency so far beyond anything on that battlefield that even the most powerful fighters present felt their conviction waver, not because they doubted themselves but because they were suddenly, viscerally reminded of what a legend looked like when he stopped holding back.

Garp stood in the crater between his son and the pirate, arms folded, Marine coat draped over his shoulders with the indifference of a man who had long stopped caring about the institution it represented. He looked at Dragon. He looked at Ajay. And with Bodhi's perception, through the Mandala Eye that was still clearing the static of the Galaxy Impact, Ajay saw something he had not expected: Garp's Haki was not just more powerful than theirs. It was in a different category entirely. If Ajay and Dragon were storm clouds, Garp was the sky that contained them.

Bodhi and Haki allow Ajay to fight Emperors. But for the legends above Emperors — especially one who might side with the opponent — the calculus collapses. You cannot outfight the sky. You can only wait for it to stop pressing down.

The fight ended. Not with surrender — with the shared recognition that continuing was no longer about Vegapunk. It was about ego, and Garp's fist had punched the ego out of every person on that island simultaneously.

In the aftermath, Dragon and Ajay spoke. Brief. Honest. Dragon acknowledged the truth in Ajay's conviction — that good intentions do not absolve the suffering they enable. Ajay acknowledged the truth in Dragon's — that knowledge, even bloodstained knowledge, can save more lives than vengeance ever will. Neither changed the other's mind. Neither expected to. But Ajay understood, with the clarity of Bodhi and the ache of a bruised body, why he could never join the Revolutionary Army: Dragon still holds Vegapunk as a friend. And Ajay cannot forgive on behalf of the dead.

The Grin at the End of the World
Operation: Red Storm ・ Egghead Archipelago — Vegapunk's Secondary Facilities, New World
Second attempt. Different opponent. Worse odds.

Ajay returned to the Egghead Archipelago — different islands, different facilities, same conviction. The research corridor Dragon had defended was intact, but Vegapunk's secondary network sprawled across dozens of islands, each one housing automated weapon forges, Seraphim developmental data, and the blueprints for technologies that had already cost millions of lives. The mission remained dismantlement. The Meridian descended with purpose and without apology.

The Red Hair Pirates were waiting.

Not ambushed — waiting. As if Shanks had known. As if that legendary Observation Haki — the kind that sees not just the future but the shape of decisions before they're made — had told him exactly when and where the Meridian would arrive. The Red Hair Pirates' ship sat in the harbour like it had been there for days. No anchors weighed. Sails furled casually. As if they were on vacation and happened to be blocking the entrance to every facility Ajay intended to destroy.

"Red-Haired" Shanks stood on the bow. One arm. One sword. And a Conqueror's Haki so immense it preceded him like a weather front — the ocean around his ship was still not because the sea was calm, but because the sea was holding its breath. Bounty: ₿4,048,900,000. One of the Four Emperors. The man who stopped a war at Marineford by showing up. Shanks did not fight often. When he did, civilizations noticed.

Behind him: Benn Beckman, the shadow with a rifle and the highest IQ in the East Blue, Haki that surpassed Admirals. Yasopp "the Chaser," sniper whose Future Sight Observation surpassed even Katakuri's — a man who had never missed a target in his life. Lucky Roux, cook, a bowling ball of muscle and Armament Haki who had been the first person to kill someone in the entire era of piracy. And the senior officers — Limejuice with his axe, Bonk Punch, Building Snake, Hongo, Gab, Monster — each one individually formidable enough to be a captain in their own right. Every single member of the crew was a Haki user. No Devil Fruits. No tricks. Just overwhelming, undiluted martial supremacy.

Shanks' reason was different from Dragon's. He didn't call Vegapunk a friend. He called him necessary. The balance of the world — the fragile equilibrium between Emperors, Marines, Revolutionaries, and the ancient forces that slept beneath the surface — required Vegapunk's knowledge to exist. Destroying it would create a vacuum that something worse would fill. Shanks had seen this pattern before. He had watched the consequences play out across decades. And he was not going to let a young pirate's righteous fury tip the scales into chaos.

Ajay heard the argument. He understood the logic. And he attacked anyway, because there comes a point where "balance" becomes another word for "acceptable suffering," and Ajay had met enough of Vegapunk's victims to know that the suffering was not acceptable to the people living through it.

The battle was the most violent thing the Hive Codex had ever recorded.

Ajay versus Shanks. Emperor against the swarm. Shanks' Conqueror's Haki was unlike anything Ajay had ever encountered — not a wall, not a wave, but a domain. Within Shanks' Haki radius, the rules of combat changed. Paramecia abilities weakened. The swarm became harder to control — insects responding sluggishly, castes misfiring, the Archivist struggling to maintain coherence as Shanks' sovereign will pressed against the hive architecture like a hand pressing against a soap bubble. This was not interference. This was denial — a Conqueror's so absolute it could reject the premise that another person's abilities should function within its presence. Ajay answered with Bodhi. The Mandala Eye cut through Shanks' domain — not resisting it, but seeing through it, mapping its structure, finding the seams. Golden Pulse read Shanks' intent a fraction before his body moved. Iridescent Ryou hardened the swarm against the suppression, each insect coated in a Haki that carried the alien frequency of Enlightenment. It was barely enough. Shanks fought with one arm and a sword, and every strike was a lecture in what mastery meant when backed by four decades of experience.

Rime versus Benn Beckman. The shadow met the sun. Beckman fought with Haki-imbued rifle rounds — each bullet carrying enough Armament to punch through Rime's thermal field and strike his body directly. His intelligence was terrifying — within the first exchange, Beckman had mapped Rime's attack patterns, predicted his thermal cycling, and begun firing where Rime would be rather than where he was. Rime answered with chaos — abandoning patterns entirely, fighting on pure chinchilla instinct and fury, his temperature fluctuating wildly and unpredictably because even Rime didn't know what he was going to do next. It was the smartest fighter in the East Blue against a walking thermal explosion who had decided that strategy was overrated. The landscape between them was a wasteland — scorched earth, melted stone, glass craters where Rime's plasma had hit the ground.

Petal versus Lucky Roux. The gentle healer against the human cannonball. Lucky Roux's "Self-Bowling" technique was devastating at close range — he curled his massive body into a ball and rolled through everything in his path, Armament Haki coating his surface until he was functionally an indestructible boulder with momentum. Petal's strength was range and precision, not close combat. She fought a retreating battle — Dryad Forms deployed as barriers and interceptors, the Briar Ankylos absorbing Roux's charges, the Nature Golem physically wrestling him to slow his momentum, Dandelion Sprites scattering to harass from every angle. Her Observation Haki was the only thing keeping her alive — Future Sight warning her when Roux's next bowling charge would break through her formation, giving her exactly enough time to reposition. Roux was laughing the entire time. So was Petal, though hers was quieter and came with flowers growing in the craters.

Tidus versus Yasopp. The builder against the sniper who never misses. Yasopp's Future Sight Observation Haki was the most advanced on any battlefield Tidus had ever stood on — the man didn't predict attacks, he pre-experienced them, seeing the future unfold with a clarity that made the present look like a rough draft. Every barrier Tidus raised, Yasopp had already identified the timing gap before the barrier finished forming. Every coral construct, every water wall, every fortification — Yasopp's bullet was already in the air, aimed at the precise millisecond when the defense would transition from building to built. Tidus had to think in terms of futures — layered barriers designed not to block the current shot but the shot after next, barrier sequences whose timing gaps were intentional traps that forced Yasopp to choose between two bad options. It was two geniuses of prediction fighting in a temporal landscape that existed three seconds ahead of reality.

The Senior Officers versus the Vanguard. The Red Hair Pirates' elite — Limejuice, Bonk Punch, Building Snake, Hongo, Gab, Monster — against the Twelve Who Stand. Both groups were tier-defying fighters. The Vanguard fought in their cute forms, Conqueror's-imbued strikes launched from bodies that looked like they belonged in a nursery. The senior officers fought with decades of Emperor-crew experience and Haki refined to razors. Harui's precision met Limejuice's axe work. Gankaku's blade met Bonk Punch's percussion-style combat. Kagami's reflections met Building Snake's coiling strikes. The Vanguard held — barely. The Red Hair officers were not individually stronger, but they were older, more experienced, and their Haki had the density that only came from years of sailing under Shanks' flag.

The Rest versus the Meridian. The remaining Red Hair Pirates — every last crew member, all of them Haki users, all of them formidable — against the Meridian, the homies, the Copplings, and the Harvest Swarm. Claude coordinated from the ship. Xylem ran logistics from the main deck, her logbook pages turning faster than they ever had. The Copplings held infrastructure. The homies held lines. But the Red Hair Pirates were an Emperor crew without a single weak link — even their newest members fought at a level that would qualify as Vice Admiral in most navies.

The island burned. Sand turned to glass under the weight of concentrated Haki. Waves split — not from impact, but from the pressure of competing Conqueror's wills pressing against the ocean itself. Clouds fled. The sky above the archipelago went clear and stayed clear, not because of Tidus' domain but because the combined Conqueror's output of everyone on that battlefield had made the atmosphere itself decide that the safest option was to get out of the way. Haki permeated every cell on the island — every stone, every grain of sand, every molecule of air vibrated with the residual frequency of sovereign wills clashing. Marine observation vessels fifty miles away reported their instruments overloading.

The Meridian's side was losing.

Not dramatically. Not catastrophically. But steadily, inevitably, the way the tide goes out — inch by inch, and then suddenly you realize the shore is very far away. Shanks was too strong. Beckman was too smart. Lucky Roux was too relentless. Yasopp was too precise. The senior officers were too experienced. The crew had no weak points. The Meridian's forces were being pushed back on every front, not routed but compressed, their operational space shrinking as the Red Hair Pirates applied pressure with the patient, overwhelming confidence of people who had been doing this for decades.

And then — at the edge, at the brink where losing becomes inevitable — the Codex evolved.

It started with Ajay.

Not V2. Not the Totality. Something different — something the Archivist had never predicted because it came not from the Sovereign's command but from the hive itself. The Mure Mure no Mi, pushed to its absolute limit against an Emperor whose Conqueror's was suppressing its function, reached deeper than it ever had before. Past the Marshal. Past the Archivist. Past the Queen's Doctrine. Into the connections themselves — the soul-tethers that linked Ajay to his companions, the resonance frequencies that connected him to every homie, every Coppling, every entity aboard the Meridian. And those connections opened.

The Hive Connection.

Not V2's forced subsumption — not the Sovereign taking control of every body. This was the inverse. Every crew member's Haki flowed into Ajay, voluntarily, instinctively, as if the hive had decided — collectively, without instruction, without the Sovereign's command — that the network should share everything. And Ajay, serving not as commander but as conduit, distributed it back outward. Rime's thermal frequency flowed through Petal. Petal's Observation clarity flowed through Tidus. Tidus' Conqueror's domain flowed through the homies. Claude's processing efficiency flowed through the Mote network and into every construct on the battlefield. Even Bodhi — the alien spiritual frequency of Enlightenment — resonated through every connection, touching every bonded entity with a golden warmth that defied classification.

Akebono rose from the Sanctuary — the Jelly Princess dividing into hundreds of shimmering copies that flooded the battlefield, each fragment carrying Tidus' barrier-water and now infused with the shared Haki of the entire network. Xylem — Xylem, the logistics construct, the clipboard-carrying conductor of supplies and schedules — felt Conqueror's Haki bloom in her chest for the first time. Her eyes went wide. Her logbook fell. And then she picked it up again and kept coordinating, because that was who she was, except now her coordination carried the weight of a sovereign will that made every instruction she gave feel like a royal decree. Even the Copplings — mechanical constructs, Dial-powered, theoretically incapable of Haki — felt the resonance through their cores, their Dial systems harmonizing with the frequency until their strikes carried Armament coating that had no mechanical origin.

All five activated Transcendence.

Simultaneously.

Rime's sand-to-glass became glass-to-crystal — every surface his Ryou touched restructured at the molecular level into crystalline formations that reflected and amplified Haki. Petal's tangible Observation expanded until it covered the entire archipelago — every living thing felt the weight of being perceived by a consciousness that had absorbed the sensory data of fifty crew members and synthesized it into omniscience. Tidus' domain-level Conqueror's cleared not just the sky but the sea — the ocean retreated from the island's shores in a perfect circle, the seabed exposed, the water afraid to return. Claude's network reached a processing density that turned every Mote into a relay station capable of independent tactical analysis — each one a miniature intelligence coordinating its section of the battlefield with the precision of a fleet admiral.

And through the Hive Connection, every crew member shared in all of it.

The Vanguard struck with Conqueror's-imbued attacks that suddenly carried the weight of the entire crew's collective will. Harui's blade rang with Rime's thermal frequency. Kagami's reflections carried Petal's botanical precision. Gankaku's steel sang with Tidus' barrier resonance. The senior officers of the Red Hair Pirates — who had been winning — suddenly found themselves fighting not twelve small warriors but twelve conduits for a unified consciousness that hit from every direction with the combined Haki of a civilization.

Shanks felt it. His grin widened.

The standstill lasted seven minutes. Seven minutes where the Red Hair Pirates — the crew whose Haki balance was considered unbreachable — were held. Not pushed back. Not defeated. But held, for seven minutes, by a crew that was objectively weaker on every individual metric but had discovered something the Red Hair Pirates had never needed: a way to become one.

And in the sixth minute, they deployed it.
