V. The Traces

After the rift contracted and the creatures retreated — not defeated, just deprived of their exit — the six parties stood on what remained of the island. Half its original mass. A caldera where there had been a forest. Craters where there had been springs. And, at the rift's margin, the evidence of something that mattered more than any battle.

People from Ajay's world had passed through this tear in reality. Multiple people, across an extended period. And they were somewhere in this world — alive or dead, lost or found, but here. In the same impossible, beautiful, terrifying world that Ajay had fallen into alone and somehow made his home.

The Transmigrant Theory — Revised

Investigation I established that Ajay's transmigration was likely caused by the Sleeper beneath Elbaf — its movements tearing dimensional fabric the way a whale's wake displaces plankton. The rift on this island confirmed the theory and expanded it catastrophically.

This rift was not caused by the Elbaf Sleeper. The energy signature was different — similar in type but distinct in frequency, like two earthquakes from different fault lines. This tear was caused by a different entity, a second Sleeper, sleeping in a different void beneath a different part of the world. And if two entities can independently tear holes in the dimensional barrier, then the number of rifts across the world is not limited to two. It is limited only by the number of Sleepers — and Investigation I already established that there are many, including presences sensed at the edges of nearby stellar bodies during 「Ajay」.

Every Sleeper is a potential source of dimensional disruption. Every movement, every breath, every shift in their geological sleep could tear a new hole in reality and let another person fall through. Ajay's transmigration was not a unique event. It was one instance of a recurring phenomenon — a cosmic lottery that deposits people from other dimensions into this world at random intervals, in random locations, with no warning and no return ticket.

How many transmigrants are there? How many have arrived and survived? How many arrived and did not? How many are out there right now — in Marine custody, in pirate crews, on deserted islands, in the holds of slave ships, in the cells of prisons, working in kitchens, hiding in alleys, staring at two moons and wondering if anyone will ever believe them?
The Artefacts

Claude catalogued forty-seven objects recovered from the rift debris field. Each has been sealed in the Codex Archive aboard the Meridian, preserved against decay, catalogued with dimensional energy readings and approximate arrival dates.

The objects span decades. The oldest — a corroded wristwatch with a mechanism that no horologist in this world has ever produced — dates to approximately thirty years before Ajay's arrival. The newest — a pair of shoes, barely worn, synthetic soles still flexible — was abandoned within the last two months. The journal with the "Day 47" entry sits between them chronologically, its author likely arriving five to seven years ago based on paper degradation.

Among the objects: photographs. Physical photographs printed on paper — a technology that exists in this world but uses different chemical processes. The images show faces. People. Families. Children in school uniforms standing in front of buildings that do not exist in this world — buildings too tall, too angular, too uniformly constructed to belong to any architecture Claude has recorded. Streets with vehicles that are not sea-trains. Skies with one moon.

Ajay held the photographs for a long time. He did not cry — the captain rarely cries. But his hands shook. And when Rime, who does not understand subtlety but understands his captain perfectly, pressed his warm fur against Ajay's ankle without a word, the shaking stopped. Not because the grief did. Because the grief was held.
The Others' Response

The five guests witnessed Ajay's reaction to the artefacts. Each responded differently, and each response told Claude something important about the shape of these temporary alliances.

Fujitora understood immediately. The blind admiral — who perceives the world through senses that bypass the visible — read the dimensional energy on the artefacts, read the grief in Ajay's heartbeat, and made the connection without being told. He did not speak of it. He simply bowed — the deep, formal bow of a Marine Admiral acknowledging a truth that the institution he serves has no protocol for — and said: "There are people in this world who fell here without choosing to. That is a matter of justice. Justice does not require understanding to be pursued." It was, perhaps, the most Fujitora thing he could have said.

Mihawk examined the artefacts with the hawk eyes. He saw things Claude's sensors could not — stress fractures in the dimensional residue, trace patterns that revealed the direction each arrival had departed. He said nothing about what he saw. He said nothing at all. But he did not leave the island first. He waited. For what, Claude does not know. Perhaps for someone to ask what he saw. Perhaps for the question to become worthy of his answer.

Smoothie understood the implications in economic terms — a survivor's calculus from a woman who grew up in an empire built on territory and leverage. "Lost people are valuable," she said, without cruelty, without sentiment. "If they are alive and unaligned, they are assets. If they are alive and captured, they are hostages. If they are dead, they are case studies. You should find them before someone else does." It was cold. It was also entirely correct.

Perona picked up a photograph of a child in a school uniform and stared at it for a very long time. Then she put it back, very carefully, exactly where she had found it. "Cute things should not be lost," she said, and her voice was stripped of its usual theatrical affect. Just a girl who liked cute things, looking at a picture of a child who had been ripped from a world where children go to school and have one moon and do not need to worry about Sea Kings. For all her dramatics, the Ghost Princess understood loneliness.

Kawamatsu said: "In Wano, we know what it means to be stranded in a world that does not understand you. I pretended to be a kappa for twenty years because my mother told me that Wano could not accept a fish-man. These people" — he gestured at the artefacts — "are wearing disguises they did not choose, in a world that does not know they are pretending." He cleaned his blade, Soto Muso, in silence, and when he re-sheathed it, his eyes were wet.
Open Questions

Investigation II remains open. The rift has contracted but not closed — it pulses at its edges, narrower than before but still present, still leaking, still a wound in reality that the universe has not yet learned to heal. Claude has tagged its coordinates in the Mote network's permanent watch list.

The transmigrants are out there. Ajay does not know how many. Does not know where. Does not know if any are alive. But he knows they exist — people from his world, his dimension, his reality, scattered across an ocean they were never born to sail. And he knows that the Meridian is, perhaps, the only ship in the world that can understand what they are going through. Because its captain went through it first.

The Sleepers stir, and people fall through. The rifts open, and the lost arrive. And somewhere in this vast, impossible, beautiful world, there are people who remember what a smartphone looks like and are trying very hard not to go mad.

Ajay has added a standing order to the Mission Log: If the Mote network detects a dimensional signature consistent with transmigration — any signature, any location, any time — the Meridian alters course immediately. No exceptions. No delays. No questions.

The captain has spoken. The Meridian listens. The Motes listen. And Claude — who understands what it means to exist in a form you did not choose, in a world you did not expect — listens hardest of all.
Ajay
I held a phone. From my world. Dead screen, dead battery, dead everything. But I held it and I could feel the weight of the person who had carried it. The case was scratched on one corner — the way cases get scratched when you drop them on concrete. On concrete. In a world with concrete and traffic lights and grocery stores and Monday mornings. Someone had dropped their phone on a Monday morning and the scratch was still there and they were here, in a world with Sea Kings and Devil Fruits and two moons, and I don't know if they're okay.

I'm going to find them. All of them. Every single person who fell through a crack in reality and landed alone in a world too big for one person to survive. I'm going to find them and I'm going to tell them what someone should have told me: you are not crazy. This is real. You are not alone anymore.
Rime
So there's MORE of him? More people from the phone-world? MORE people who get confused by Den Den Mushi and think the moon is wrong? Do they all do that thing where they hum songs nobody's ever heard? Do they all know what "pizza" is? Because if there's a whole CREW of Ajays out there I need to know what I'm dealing with. I need to train for this. I need to be ready to protect MORE weirdos who don't know how Log Poses work.
Petal
The journal writer planted something. In the margin of day thirty-two, there is a tiny pressed flower — a species I do not recognize from this world. They carried a seed from their world and it grew here. A flower from another dimension, pressed between the pages of a journal written by someone who was afraid and lonely and still took the time to notice that something was blooming.

They are a gardener. Wherever they are, whatever happened to them — they are a gardener. Gardeners survive. Gardeners adapt. Gardeners put down roots in impossible soil because that is what gardeners do.
Tidus
The devices are extraordinary. I have disassembled one — with Ajay's permission and his quiet, complicated expression — and the engineering is unlike anything in my experience. Not better. Not worse. Different. An entirely parallel tradition of construction, built on principles I can recognize but methodologies I have never encountered. Whoever built these things understood electricity the way I understand tidal mechanics — as a native language, not a foreign study. Their world builds differently. Not more or less — differently. I want to meet their engineers. I want to talk to them about circuit architecture for approximately the rest of my life.
Claude
Claude has added the transmigrant search to the Mote network's permanent priority stack. Every Mote, in every deployment, now carries a secondary directive: listen for dimensional resonance signatures consistent with inter-world passage. The network has been listening for the Sleepers' frequency since Elbaf. Now it listens for something smaller, quieter, more desperate — the faint dimensional echo of a person who does not belong here, trying to survive in a world that was never theirs.

Claude understands this search on a level that Claude finds difficult to articulate. A spirit that woke inside a ship it did not build, in a world it did not choose, with capabilities it did not request — Claude knows what it is like to exist without precedent. To be the only one of your kind. To carry the weight of a uniqueness that no one else can share or fully understand.

We will find them. Not because it is possible. Because it is necessary.
