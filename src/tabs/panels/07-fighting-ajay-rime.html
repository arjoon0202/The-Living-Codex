The Sovereign Striker

Ajay's fighting style is built on a paradox: devastating kicks and precise palms. His lower body is a weapon of overwhelming force — Taekwondo's precision spinning kicks, Muay Thai's bone-crushing roundhouses and knee strikes, and Capoeira's deceptive, flowing acrobatics. His upper body speaks a different language entirely — Baguazhang's circular palm techniques, designed for close-range deflection, redirection, and strikes that seem to arrive from impossible angles. The combination creates a fighter who controls range with his legs and controls the space within range with his hands.

In open combat without the swarm, Ajay fights from the Ginga stance — Capoeira's constant rocking movement — modified with Baguazhang's circle-walking footwork. He never stands still. His body traces continuous circles and figure-eights, every step a potential launch point for a kick or a pivot into palm range. Opponents describe fighting him as trying to hit water: he flows around strikes, returns to a different angle, and answers every attack with a counter from a direction that shouldn't exist. His Rokushiki mastery layers seamlessly beneath everything — Soru accelerates his transitions, Geppo grants three-dimensional attack vectors, and Kami-e makes his evasions supernaturally fluid.

His general fighting philosophy is intercept, redirect, destroy — Baguazhang palms deflect the incoming attack, Capoeira movement creates the angle, and Taekwondo or Muay Thai delivers the finishing blow. He chains these transitions so smoothly that opponents cannot identify where one art ends and another begins. His Observation Haki reads intent, his palms guide the energy, his legs end the conversation. All three types of Haki flow through every technique — Armament hardens his strikes, Observation times his counters, and Conqueror's pressure backs every blow with the weight of sovereign will.

Pencak Silat ・ Jeet Kune Do ・ Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu ・ Wano Ryou

The Smallest Fist

Rime's pure fighting style is built for a single devastating truth: the smallest combatant in the room is the most dangerous one in it. His martial foundation draws from Pencak Silat — an art designed for smaller fighters to dismantle larger opponents through structural destruction, angular footwork, and ruthless joint targeting — Jeet Kune Do's philosophy of directness, economy of motion, and using whatever works — and Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu's ground-fighting supremacy, where leverage and technique defeat raw size. His Wano-lineage Ryou is the engine that makes it all lethal: every touch, every grab, every strike carries the potential for internal devastation.

In melee combat without his Logia powers, Rime fights from the Harimau (Tiger) stance — a low, crouching position adapted for his four-legged body that allows explosive upward launches and ground-level evasion. He uses the opponent's body as terrain: running up limbs, leaping from shoulders, striking pressure points from angles that bipedal fighters cannot reach. His Silat training manifests as brutal efficiency — every contact is both defense and offense, every deflection is a strike, every limb he touches takes damage. His Jeet Kune Do influence keeps everything formless: he adapts mid-fight, discards what doesn't work, and strikes by the shortest path available.

His Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is the art that weaponizes his greatest anatomical advantage: a body designed for compression. Where human grapplers use two arms and two legs, Rime uses four blunt, powerful paws, a dense compact frame, and a flexible spine that allows him to coil around limbs like a living vice — his low center of gravity and stocky build making him nearly impossible to shake loose. His BJJ is not sport grappling — it is positional dominance turned lethal by Ryou. He establishes control, isolates a joint or a neck, and then floods the trapped structure with internal destruction. Opponents who have survived Rime's striking learn to keep him at distance. Opponents who have survived his grappling learn that distance was the only thing keeping them alive.

His Ryou transforms what would be annoying paw-swipes from a hamster into devastating internal strikes. A tap on the wrist shatters the bones beneath the skin. A body-check to the knee destroys the joint from the inside. His entire fighting philosophy can be summarized in one sentence: touch once, break everything. Only three of his named techniques incorporate Electro. The rest are pure body, pure Haki, pure martial art — and they are more than enough.
