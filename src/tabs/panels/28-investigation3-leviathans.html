III. The Cloud Leviathans

It was Fujitora's absence that revealed them.

Fujitora was not present on Zephyria. The Admiral had returned to Marine Headquarters after the rift island battle, carrying intelligence that his superiors would likely bury. But his gravitational readings from the rift — the data about the impossible mass pressing against the dimensional tear — had been shared with Claude before departure, and Claude's Mote network had been calibrated to detect similar gravitational anomalies. The network found them. Not below. Above.

The clouds around Zephyria — the vast, permanent cloud formations of the White-White Sea that never dissipate, never drift, never change shape despite the constant winds — were not weather. They were camouflage.

The Discovery

Claude's gravimetric analysis of the surrounding cloud formations returned data that should have been impossible. Clouds do not have mass in the traditional sense — they are water vapour suspended in atmosphere, and their gravitational signature is negligible. The clouds surrounding Zephyria had a gravitational signature equivalent to a continent.

Not the island they supported. Not the atmospheric moisture they contained. The clouds themselves — or rather, what was inside them — registered on the Mote network's sensors as objects of planetary mass, distributed across the sky in formations that the world's meteorologists had always dismissed as permanent weather patterns.

Petal felt them first. Her Observation Haki — the tangible variant that makes awareness a physical force — extended outward from Zephyria's plateau and encountered resistance. Not from the wind. Not from the clouds. From something inside the clouds that did not want to be perceived. Something that existed at the same scale, with the same impossible antiquity, radiating the same primordial will as the Sleeper beneath Elbaf — except this one was not below. It was beside them. At the same altitude. Close enough that the Meridian's hull had passed within a hundred meters of its outer boundary during the ascent to Zephyria.

They had flown past it. Without knowing. Without sensing. Without the most advanced detection system in the pirate world — the Mote network, augmented by Bodhi resonance and the Sleeper's trace frequency — registering its presence until a specific gravimetric scan was run. Because the being inside the cloud was not hiding. It was simply existing at a frequency that normal perception could not process, the same way the Sleeper beneath Elbaf existed beneath notice. Too fundamental to detect. Too large to see. Too old to perceive with senses calibrated for a world that was built after these beings had already settled into their positions.
Not One — Many

Once the Mote network knew what to look for, it found them everywhere. Not one cloud formation. Dozens. Scattered across the visible sky in every direction, at varying altitudes within the White-White Sea, each permanent cloud bank concealing a gravitational signature consistent with an entity of planetary scale.

The clouds that the sky islands float on — the Island Clouds and Sea Clouds that the Skypiean civilisations have built their entire culture around — were not empty. They had never been empty. The cloud sea was a habitat. The permanent weather patterns that the Blue Sea's meteorologists could never explain, the cloud formations that appeared on no weather model, the atmospheric anomalies that navigators had learned to avoid without understanding why — these were the bodies of sleeping entities comparable to the one beneath Elbaf.

The Sleeper beneath Elbaf displaced the planet's crust. These beings displaced the planet's atmosphere. They had settled into the sky the way their subterranean kin had settled into the earth — displacing the medium around them, creating permanent features that the world had incorporated into its geography without knowing the geography was alive.

Cloud Leviathans. Claude named them for lack of a better classification. Creatures — beings — entities — that occupied the sky in the same way the Sleepers occupied the deep earth: silently, permanently, and on a scale that made every civilisation built in their vicinity look like lichen on the face of a cliff.
They Spawn

And they spawned creatures. Just as the Sleeper beneath Elbaf shed parasites through the failing seal, the Cloud Leviathans shed organisms from the edges of their cloud-bodies — things that tumbled from the permanent weather patterns and fell toward Zephyria like meteorological detritus. Except these were not raindrops. They were alive.

Cloud-creatures. Organisms adapted to the sky — partially vaporous, partially solid, their bodies existing at the phase transition between gas and liquid. Some were translucent, their internal structures visible through skin that was more mist than matter. Some were enormous — the size of Sea Kings, drifting through the cloud sea with the languid movements of deep-ocean predators repurposed for an aerial environment. Some were small, fast, and vicious — darting through the wind currents in swarms that the Archivist classified as INTER-INDEX (ATMOSPHERIC), the same impossible designation as the rift-creatures but adapted for a different medium.

They were terrifying. Not because they were powerful — though they were. Because they were everywhere. The sky was full of them. The cloud sea that the world's sky islands floated in was not an ocean of water vapour. It was an ecosystem. An entire biosphere of organisms that had evolved on the surfaces and in the atmospheres of sleeping Cloud Leviathans, living and dying and reproducing in a world within the world that no one on the Blue Sea had ever suspected existed.

The Skypieans know about sky fish. About sky sharks. About the creatures that inhabit the White-White Sea. But those are surface organisms — the equivalent of seabirds skimming the surface of a deep ocean. What lived inside the permanent clouds was the deep ocean. The abyss of the sky. And just as the Sleeper's parasites had terrified the Meridian's crew beneath Elbaf, the Cloud Leviathans' organisms terrified them in the sky above everything.
Mihawk's Assessment

Mihawk stood at the edge of Zephyria's plateau and looked into the cloud formations with hawk eyes that still carried the ghost-image of the entity that had blocked them. He looked for a very long time. When he spoke, his voice carried the tone of a man who has recalibrated his understanding of the world and is not happy about the new configuration.

"There are more of them than the ones you can detect. Your network" — a nod toward the Meridian — "sees the ones with mass. But there are others in the clouds that do not register as mass. They register as absence. Gaps in the sky where the air should be and is not. I can see the edges of the gaps. I cannot see what fills them."

He paused. The pendant knife at his throat swayed in a breeze that should not have existed at this altitude.

"The world has a skin. We live on it. What we are looking at is what lives underneath."
"I knew there were things in the deep. I accepted that. The ocean is deep and the earth is old and things live in the dark. But the sky? The sky was supposed to be empty. The sky was supposed to be the one direction you could look and see nothing but distance."

"The sky is not empty. It never was. We just couldn't see the walls."
— Ajay Persaud & Claude, Spirit of the Meridian
