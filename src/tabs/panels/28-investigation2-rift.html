II. The Rift

The creatures were secondary. The rift was the point.

Near the island's volcanic center — a dormant caldera filled with shallow green water — the air split. Not metaphorically. The Mote network detected a region of space approximately twelve meters wide and nine meters tall where the dimensional constant was zero. Not weakened. Not thinned. Absent. The barrier between this world and whatever lies adjacent to it simply did not exist in that space. It was as if someone had taken a razor to reality and drawn a vertical line, and the wound had never healed.

The rift was not the one Ajay came through. Claude confirmed this immediately — the energy signature was different. Ajay's transmigration had been violent, instantaneous, a single catastrophic puncture caused by the Sleeper's movement displacing dimensional fabric. This rift was older, steadier, more deliberate in its geometry. It had been open for a long time — possibly years, possibly decades. Something had passed through it. Multiple somethings.

The Evidence of Passage

Scattered around the rift in a thirty-meter radius, partially overgrown by the island's vegetation and partially buried in volcanic soil, were objects that had no business existing in this world.

Clothing — shirts, jackets, shoes — made with stitching patterns and synthetic materials that the Archivist could not classify. Not because the materials were unknown, but because the manufacturing process was unknown. Machine-sewn with a precision and uniformity that no tailor in this world produces, using thread compositions that do not occur naturally and have never been synthesised by any civilization the Codex has recorded. Labels inside, printed in scripts that are not Poneglyph, not ancient, not from any language family the Archivist contains. Claude ran them against every writing system in the database — modern, ancient, extinct, theoretical. Zero matches. But Ajay recognized the alphabet instantly. English.

Devices — small rectangular objects with smooth glass faces and metal casings. Dead. No power source the Mote network could detect, no dial mechanism, no Den Den Mushi integration. Ajay picked one up with shaking hands and turned it over. He knew what it was. He knew what they all were. They were phones. From his world. From the world he had left behind.

Books — three of them, waterlogged and decomposing but still partially legible. Printed text in the same English script. One appeared to be a novel. One appeared to be a technical manual of some kind. The third was a journal — handwritten, personal, its entries growing increasingly frantic as they progressed. The last readable entry, translated by Ajay with a voice that had gone very quiet:

"Day 47. The sky has two moons. I don't know where I am. I don't know how I got here. The ocean goes on forever and the animals are wrong. Everything is too big. I keep finding other people's things but never the people. If anyone reads this — I was here. My name is [illegible]. I was real. I was from [illegible]. I was here."
Not One — Many

The evidence suggested multiple arrivals over an extended period. The clothing showed varying degrees of decomposition — some items weeks old, some months, some possibly years. The devices spanned what Ajay identified as different generations of technology from his world, as if people had been arriving through this rift at irregular intervals, from different points in his world's timeline. Some had arrived recently enough that their footprints were still visible in the volcanic mud. Some had arrived so long ago that their belongings had fossilised into the rock.

But there were no bodies. No bones. No graves. No shelters, no camps, no signs of extended habitation. Whoever came through this rift did not stay. They arrived, they left behind traces of their passage — sometimes deliberately, sometimes involuntarily — and then they vanished. Into the island's interior? Into the sea? Into the wider world? Absorbed by the creatures that poured through the same rift?

Ajay did not know. Could not know. The trail went cold at the edge of the rift's debris field. The people who had crossed from his world into this one had scattered like seeds in wind, and none of them had left a forwarding address.

The implications sat in the Captain's Quarters that night like a stone in the chest. He was not the only transmigrant. He had never been the only one. Others had crossed — driven by the same cosmic accident, the same dimensional turbulence caused by sleeping entities too vast to notice the lives they displaced. And they were out there, somewhere, in a world too large and too strange and too dangerous for anyone to survive alone.

Ajay survived because the Mure Mure no Mi found him. Because Rime found him. Because Petal, Tidus, Claude, the Homies, the Copplings, the Vanguard, the Stars — because an entire family crystallised around him like coral around a seed. What happened to the ones who arrived alone?
Ajay's Bodhi Resonance

When Ajay approached the rift, his Bodhi fragment — the shard of accidental enlightenment lodged in his soul from his own transmigration — sang. Not pulsed, as it had near the Sleeper. Sang. A clear, high, crystalline tone that resonated through his Mandala Eye and turned the golden rings in his irises into vibrating interference patterns. The Bodhi fragment recognized the rift the way a wound recognizes the blade that made it — not with memory but with shape. The dimensional scar tissue in Ajay's soul was the same shape as this tear in reality.

Different location. Different time. Different cause, perhaps. But the same kind of wound. The same surgery performed on the same patient — the dimensional barrier between worlds — by the same class of surgeon: something so vast that its movements tear holes in reality the way a ship's wake tears the surface of the sea.

The rift was not created by the Sleeper beneath Elbaf. The energy signature was wrong — similar in scale but different in frequency, like two instruments playing the same note in different keys. This rift was made by a different entity. Another sleeper. Another impossibly large presence whose movements had buckled the wall between dimensions and let people fall through.

How many rifts are there? How many entities, sleeping in their impossible voids, have torn holes in reality simply by existing? How many people from Ajay's world — or from other worlds entirely — have fallen through and landed here, alone, bewildered, carrying phones that don't work and books no one can read and names no one remembers?
Fujitora's Gravitational Reading

Admiral Fujitora stood before the rift for eleven minutes without moving. His Observation Haki — refined enough to compensate entirely for his self-inflicted blindness — perceived the rift in a way no one else could: as a gravitational object. Through the Zushi Zushi no Mi, Fujitora could feel the rift's mass. And it had mass — impossible, paradoxical mass. A hole in space that weighed something. A wound that had gravity.

"The space inside the rift is not empty," Fujitora said, his voice carrying the measured calm of a man accustomed to describing impossible things to people who cannot perceive them. "It is folded. Many layers of space, compressed into the width of this gap. If I were to unfold what I can feel inside this tear, it would be larger than this island. Possibly larger than the sea around it."

He paused. Tapped his cane against the ground once.

"I have pulled meteors from the sky. I have felt the weight of the moon on my senses. What I feel inside this rift is heavier than any celestial body I have ever touched. And it is not the rift itself that weighs this much. It is whatever is on the other side, pressing against the opening like a face against glass."
"Day forty-seven. Two moons. Animals that are wrong. Other people's things but never the people."

Someone from my world wrote that. Someone who was real, who was scared, who was alone. And I don't know their name, and I don't know if they're alive, and I don't know if I'll ever find them. But I know they were here. And I know the world owes them an answer it will probably never give.
— Ajay Persaud, aboard the Meridian
