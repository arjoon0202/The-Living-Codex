I. The Reunion

Three weeks after the Rift. The Mote network, which had been listening for dimensional anomalies with the obsessive focus of a system that has learned to expect the impossible, found one in the last place anyone would have thought to look: the sky.

A sky island. Uncharted, unreachable by conventional navigation — floating in the White-White Sea at an altitude that even Log Pose readings dismissed as atmospheric noise. Claude's network detected it not by its position but by its frequency: the same dimensional resonance signature that the rift on the nameless island had emitted, except this signal was coming from thirty thousand feet above the ocean's surface. Something up there was vibrating at the frequency of broken reality. Something up there had torn the same wound between worlds that the Meridian had just spent fourteen hours fighting to close.

The Meridian flew. That is, after all, what the Meridian was built to do.

The Sky Island — Zephyria

Claude named it Zephyria — after the west wind — because the island existed in a perpetual state of gentle, warm breeze that felt completely wrong at thirty thousand feet. It was beautiful. Cloud-rimmed, ringed with the shimmering veil of Island Cloud formations, its interior a lush plateau of sky-vegetation — the pale, floating flora that grows only in the White-White Sea's unique atmospheric conditions. Ancient ruins dotted the plateau — not Shandorian, not Birkan, not Skypiean. Something else. Something older. Structures made with materials that the Archivist classified as EXTRA-DIMENSIONAL COMPOSITE — matter that existed in this world's physics but had clearly been assembled using principles from somewhere else.

The ruins were not made by anyone from the Blue Sea. They were not made by anyone from the Sky Sea. They were made by people who had arrived — transmigrants, dimensional refugees — who had landed on this sky island and, unlike the terrified journal-writer on the rift island, had stayed long enough to build.
The Guests — Again

They were not alone. The Meridian crested Zephyria's cloud rim to find that the gathering had already begun.

Prince Loki stood at the centre of the ruins, Ragnir resting against his shoulder, the squirrel spirit in the hammerhead pressed flat and listening — its ears swivelled toward the ground, toward the clouds, toward something in every direction at once. Loki had followed Ragnir's instinct — the messenger between worlds drawn to this place the way a compass needle is drawn to magnetic north. He did not know why. The hammer knew. The hammer always knows where the passages are.

Boa Hancock had arrived by serpent-ship, her Kuja pirates maintaining a defensive perimeter around the island's southern approach. She had not been invited. She had not been asked. She had come because the Pirate Empress, since Elbaf, had been unable to stop thinking about the Sleeper. About the thing her Conqueror's Haki had recoiled from. About the first and only entity she had ever encountered that could not be affected by desire, by love, by the fundamental mechanism of her Devil Fruit. Hancock had spent her life as the most beautiful woman in the world — a title that had been both armour and prison. And now she had encountered something that made beauty irrelevant. Something that predated the concept. She needed to understand it. She needed to understand what beauty is in a world where things like that exist.

Mihawk arrived alone, his coffin-shaped raft pulled alongside the Meridian as if the World's Strongest Swordsman had been waiting for the flying ship to show him the way up. He had. Since the nameless island — since the moment something had blocked his vision — Dracule Mihawk had been unable to look at the horizon without seeing the afterimage of what had briefly filled his sight. The hawk eyes remembered. They remembered too well. Mihawk was here because the eyes that see everything had seen something they could not forget, and the only cure for a swordsman's obsession is to understand what the blade cannot cut.

Perona floated beside Mihawk in astral projection form, her physical body tucked safely aboard the coffin-raft. She had followed Mihawk because she always follows Mihawk, but her ghosts were behaving differently this time. Up here, in the White-White Sea, her Negative Hollows were resonating — vibrating at a frequency they had never produced before, as if the dimensional energy saturating the sky island was sympathetically exciting the spectral matter that composed them. The ghosts were more potent here. More responsive. As if the boundary between the living and the dead, like the boundary between dimensions, was thinner in this place.
The Pact Renewed

Five guests. Four of whom had fought alongside the Meridian before — at Elbaf, at the rift, or both. One — Hancock — who had her own reasons for returning. The awkwardness of Elbaf's wordless alliance had softened into something that none of them would call trust but all of them recognised as precedent. They had fought together. They had survived together. They had seen the same impossible things and carried the same impossible knowledge. That shared weight had created something — not a bond, not yet, but a geometry. A shape that these disparate people occupied together, like planets in an orbit that none of them had chosen but none of them could escape.

Ajay grinned at all of them. The golden grin — Bodhi-bright, Mandala-ringed, the grin that makes serious people uncomfortable because it suggests that the universe is fundamentally hilarious and only some people are in on the joke.

"Same monsters," he said. "Different altitude. Let's go."

Loki laughed — a sound like a mountain coughing. Hancock pretended not to smile. Mihawk's hawk eyes narrowed in what might, generously, be interpreted as amusement. Perona declared the sky island's ruins "insufficiently cute" and demanded improvements. And somewhere in the Constellation Room, forty-six toys turned their faces toward the sky, and the warm, magical playroom hummed with a will that exceeded measurement.
