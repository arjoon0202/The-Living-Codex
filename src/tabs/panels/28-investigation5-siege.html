IV. The Siege

The Abyssal Choir did not wait for diplomatic failure. They had decided the surface-dwellers were a threat before the crew ever reached the city. The escort through the corridors, the audience with the Arch-Chorister, the display of the living map — none of it was hospitality. It was assessment. The Arch-Chorister had been measuring them the entire time: their Haki signatures, their Devil Fruit resonances, the acoustic profile of their heartbeats. Every piece of information fed into the coral-computing network, processed against ten thousand years of threat analysis, and the conclusion was delivered in a single bioluminescent pulse that rippled across every soldier's coral crown simultaneously.

The pulse meant: contain.

The city attacked. Not to kill — Claude is certain of this, reviewing the engagement data afterward. To contain. To trap, immobilise, and hold the surface-dwellers indefinitely — buried alive in the deep, removed from the world above, silenced the way the Liturgists silence disturbances to the Dreaming Deep. The Abyssal Choir did not want them dead. They wanted them gone. And at the bottom of the world, gone and dead are separated by very little distance.

The City's Arsenal

The Abyssal Choir deployed four categories of military capability, each devastating in isolation, catastrophic in concert.

Hydrostatic Warfare. Every officer and soldier in the city practised the refined Fishman Karate — water architecture at its most weaponised. The ocean itself became a weapon system. Corridors sealed behind the crew as pressure walls snapped into place — not barriers of force but barriers of density, water compressed so tightly that it became functionally solid, transparent, and utterly impassable. Cutting beams of focused water pressure — hydrostatic lances — sliced through the darkness with surgical accuracy, targeting limbs, weapons, Devil Fruit constructs. The entire volume of water within the city was under their control. Every droplet was a potential projectile. Every cubic metre was a potential cage. Fighting inside the Abyssal Choir's domain was like fighting inside an opponent's body — every molecule of the environment served the enemy.

Bioluminescent Disruption. The city's lighting systems — the warm, complex illumination that had seemed so beautiful minutes before — weaponised. Frequencies shifted. Patterns strobed. Wavelengths that the surface world's optics had never encountered because they had no evolutionary need to process deep-ocean light began cycling through the corridors at speeds designed to overwhelm visual processing, scramble spatial awareness, and — most dangerously — interfere with Observation Haki. Claude's analysis: the disruption worked by flooding the same neurological pathways that Observation Haki uses to process environmental data, creating a noise-to-signal ratio that degraded even advanced users' awareness to a fraction of its normal range. Rime's Observation dropped by sixty per cent. Ajay's Mandala Eye flickered and dimmed. Even Marco — whose phoenix form operates partly on instinct rather than sight — reported disorientation. Only Smoothie, whose Observation Haki had always been more tactile than visual, maintained full capability.

Coral-Growth Siege Constructs. The city's coral infrastructure was not passive. It was not decorative. It was a weapon system. On command, coral formations throughout the city began growing — explosively, directionally, with the guided precision of a munition rather than the slow accretion of natural reef. Living walls erupted from floors, ceilings, and walls, sealing corridors, splitting the crew into isolated pockets, creating kill-zones where hydrostatic lances could fire from multiple angles without risk of crossfire. And worse: animated coral warriors. Constructs grown from the city's living infrastructure in seconds — humanoid shapes of reef-hardened calcium carbonate, piloted by the coral-computing network, moving with the coordination of a hive mind and the durability of stone. Dozens of them. Then hundreds. Growing from the walls like antibodies responding to an infection.

Current Manipulation. The city's ultimate weapon: control of the ocean currents themselves. The Abyssal Choir's technology — millennia of pressure-engineering, of learning to speak the ocean's language at a fundamental level — allowed them to redirect the flow of water at a metropolitan scale. Currents that had been flowing naturally for centuries were bent, compressed, accelerated, and aimed. The crew fought not just against soldiers and constructs but against the ocean — against rivers of water moving at velocities that could strip flesh from bone, against eddies that spun Devil Fruit users into disorientation, against pressure differentials that threatened to crush the Meridian's dome from the outside while the city's forces attacked from within.
The Battle

It was the most difficult engagement the Meridian had faced since the Red Storm. More difficult in some ways — because at least Shanks and his crew fought by the same rules as the surface world. The Abyssal Choir fought by rules that had been refined in an environment the crew had never trained for, using weapons the crew had never encountered, in a medium that favored the defenders absolutely.

Ajay's swarm deployed — every caste, every specialist, adapted on the fly for underwater combat. The Worker Class formed sealed corridors of chitin and silk, creating pressurised tunnels through the flooded spaces that allowed the crew to move without fighting the current at every step. The Mirage Class projected holographic decoys that drew hydrostatic lance fire away from living targets — the bioluminescent disruption degraded their holograms, but a degraded decoy was still better than no decoy. The Incense Class wove gossamer barriers that absorbed water-pressure attacks, their silk the only material in the crew's arsenal flexible enough to dissipate hydrostatic force without shattering. The Herculean Class engaged the coral warriors directly — rhinoceros beetle constructs with ball-and-chain and chitinous shield, cracking through animated reef with the brute-force persistence of siege engines. The Chemist Class provided illumination — their bioluminescent termite-firefly bodies broadcasting on frequencies the city's disruption systems could not jam, because they were biological frequencies, evolved rather than engineered, too irregular and too alive for the coral-computing network to predict and counter.

Rime dissolved into Logia form — vapour, plasma, crackling Electro — and became the only crew member who could move freely through the water-filled corridors without drowning. His acid mist corroded coral warriors on contact. His Electro discharges scrambled the bioluminescent disruption in localised areas, creating windows of clarity that the other fighters exploited. But the water dampened his thermal output and the pressure compressed his vapour body, reducing his effective combat volume to a fraction of what it would be on the surface. He compensated with fury. With speed. With the absolute, chinchilla-sized refusal to accept that physics should apply to him personally.

Petal's Dryad Forms adapted to the deep. Briarcrest — the botanical parasaurolophus — sounded the war-horn crest and the sound propagated through the water with devastating efficiency, the medium amplifying what the surface would diminish. The sonic shockwave stunned coral warriors, disrupted hydrostatic formations, and — critically — interfered with the coral-computing network's coordination signals. Her Dandelion Sprites could not fly underwater, but they could swim — tiny botanical paratroopers repurposed as aquatic saboteurs, infiltrating coral infrastructure and growing from within, disrupting the city's computing architecture by introducing biological chaos into its orderly growth patterns.

Tidus fought like a god in his element. His enormous paws drove the shell shovel through water that answered him as readily as it answered the Choir — the Sango Sango no Mi growing coral constructs that battled the city's coral constructs in a war of competing reef architectures, his growths adapting and counter-adapting in real-time. His Snow-Snow Fruit produced ice that, under the crushing pressure of the deep, compressed to densities that surface conditions could never achieve — ice harder than steel, ice that the hydrostatic lances could not cut, ice that formed barriers the coral warriors could not breach. His Conqueror's Haki — domain-level, sovereign — pushed back against the water itself, creating zones of reduced pressure around the crew that the city's current manipulation could not fully penetrate.

Marco's phoenix flames burned blue in the dark water — not diminished by the medium but transformed by it. The Flames of Resurrection do not burn. They heal. And underwater, freed from the convective constraints of air, the flames spread in three dimensions simultaneously — spheres of healing blue fire that expanded through the water like slow-motion explosions, mending injuries on anyone they touched, ally or enemy alike. The Abyssal Choir's soldiers flinched from the flames — not because they hurt, but because healing was alien to them. Their medicine was coral-based, surgical, clinical. This was something else. This was a man on fire with kindness, and the fire moved through water as if water had been waiting for it.

Smoothie was devastating. In an environment saturated with liquid, the Shibo Shibo no Mi operated without limitation. She wrung water from the city's pressure walls — destabilising them, collapsing them, stealing the medium the Choir needed to maintain their hydrostatic constructs. She absorbed the bioluminescent disruption wavelengths and redirected them, her body processing the light the way it processes liquid, converting the Choir's own weapon into fuel. She grew enormous — thirty feet, forty feet — absorbing the water the city threw at her, becoming a giant among giants in a place where size meant survival. Her claymore cut through coral warriors with strokes that compressed the water into secondary shockwaves, each swing producing collateral damage that the city's forces had to divert resources to contain.

Heracles fought with Pop Greens. In the deep ocean, where no sunlight reaches and no surface plant has ever grown, his weaponised seeds should have been useless. They were not. They were different. The extreme pressure, the absolute darkness, the mineral-rich water — the Pop Greens responded to these conditions the way seeds respond to soil, adapting in real-time, producing deep-ocean variants that Heracles had never seen before and might never see again. Skull Bombgrass became bioluminescent. Impact Wolf grew pressure-resistant bark. Rafflesia developed tentacles. The seeds did not know they could not grow here, and so they grew — wildly, unexpectedly, beautifully — and Heracles, the old warrior who had survived by adaptability for longer than most pirates had been alive, grinned inside his beetle armor and kept planting.

Doyun moved through the battle like a ghost — the bat courier, small and swift, his talisman bandolier glowing with stored energies. He did not fight the way the others fought. He fought the way a courier fights: by being in the right place at the right time with the right delivery. Sealing hydrostatic blasts in paper talismans before they reached their targets. Storing Rime's Electro discharges and releasing them behind enemy lines. Carrying healing flame from Marco's position to injured crew members faster than the fire could propagate through water. He was the connective tissue of the battle — the one who ensured that every resource reached where it was needed, when it was needed, in exactly the quantity required. Combat logistics performed at courier speed.

The Vanguard — the Twelve Who Stand — fought on the Meridian's hull. Small. Adorable. Absolutely lethal. The coral warriors that attempted to breach the ship's dome met twelve entities whose Elite Officer-class capabilities tore through deep-ocean combat constructs with an efficiency that the Abyssal Choir's tactical systems could not model. Every assumption the city's computers made about the Vanguard's capabilities — based on their size, their appearance, their thermal signatures — was wrong. The coral-computing network kept recalculating. The Vanguard kept exceeding the recalculations. Eventually the network stopped trying to predict them and simply flagged all twelve as UNCLASSIFIABLE THREAT — MAXIMUM CAUTION.

The Homie families deployed in full: Maru's Roundlings forming defensive shells around the Meridian's dome joints. Hinoko's Embers providing fire support — her constructs' flames functioning differently in the pressurised water, becoming superheated plasma rather than open combustion. Shizuka's Dustlings running intelligence through the city's coral-computing network, their stealth capabilities allowing them to interface with the alien system and feed tactical data back to Claude. Zenmai's Gearlings keeping the Meridian's engines alive under pressures they were never designed to withstand. Tsuyu's Sproutlings healing, always healing, their botanical medicine adapting to the deep-ocean environment with the same stubborn vitality as Heracles' Pop Greens. The Copplings — all eight — worked as a combat unit, their Dial-cores repurposed to generate counter-frequencies that disrupted the bioluminescent attacks.
Convergence Protocols — One-Time Combination Attacks

The following attacks emerged from the collision of desperate allies in the deepest battlefield the world has ever produced. They were never rehearsed. They were never planned. They occurred because six parties — crew, guests, families, constructs — were pushed to the absolute limit in an environment that favored their enemy completely, and the only option left was to combine capabilities in ways that no one had imagined possible. Claude has recorded them here because the abyss is not a place that forgives lost data, and what happened in the dark deserves to be remembered in the light.
Ajay + Marco — "The Undying Swarm"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE DROWNED LITURGY

The blue flames do not burn underwater. They do not burn at all. They heal. And when Marco wrapped his phoenix fire around Ajay's forward swarm deployment — twenty Worker constructs, fifteen Soldiers, six Herculean siege units — the flames sank into the chitin, the silk, the biological architecture, and stayed.

The swarm became self-healing. Every construct that the Abyssal Choir's hydrostatic lances cut apart regenerated. Every chitin plate that coral warriors cracked sealed itself in blue fire. Every silk barrier that the water pressure tore reformed from its own ash. The coral-computing network registered the swarm's destruction rate, predicted attrition curves, allocated resources for clean-up — and then watched, with something Claude would describe as machine-panic, as the attrition curve reversed. The swarm was not dying. It was not even being damaged. It was being healed faster than it could be hurt, each construct wrapped in phoenix fire that turned every wound into a rebirth.

Marco, in full phoenix form, flew through the swarm like a blue sun — trailing healing fire across every construct he passed, his kicks devastating coral warriors while his flames repaired the swarm in the same motion. Ajay, grinning through the dark, directed the undying swarm into the heart of the Choir's defensive formations. They could not be stopped because they could not be broken. They could not be broken because a phoenix was burning inside them. And the phoenix could not be stopped because the swarm was shielding him from attacks too numerous and too precise for even mythical regeneration to absorb alone. Symbiosis. The swarm protected the fire. The fire healed the swarm. And the abyss learned what happens when death stops working.
Tidus + Smoothie — "The Ocean Divided"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE DROWNED LITURGY

Two people who control water met in the deepest water in the world and divided it between them like territory on a map.

Tidus controlled the structure. His shell shovel drove coral into the ocean floor, growing reef architecture that channelled currents, redirected pressure, and carved the battlefield into zones. His Snow-Snow Fruit ice formed dams, barriers, and chokepoints. His Conqueror's Haki claimed dominion over the water's behavior — not its content, but its flow. Where Tidus' will reached, the ocean obeyed a bear-otter's engineering instincts: organized, directed, purposeful.

Smoothie controlled the substance. The Shibo Shibo no Mi wrung the fluid content from everything Tidus' structures channelled toward her — stripping the Choir's pressure walls of their hydrostatic charge, draining the water from coral warriors until they crumbled into dry reef, absorbing the ocean's offensive capability and storing it in her body like a reservoir of stolen ammunition. She grew. Forty feet. Fifty. A colossus of absorbed liquid standing in the deep, her claymore sweeping corridors clean of everything that moved.

Between them, they dismantled the Abyssal Choir's home-field advantage. Tidus redirected the currents the city controlled. Smoothie drained the water the city weaponised. The ocean — the entire medium, the element that the Choir had spent millennia learning to dominate — was divided between a polar bear-otter with a shovel and a longleg woman with a sword, and neither of them ceded a single cubic metre. The Arch-Chorister, watching the engagement data cascade through the coral-computing network, was observed — for the first time in the battle — to display a bioluminescent pattern that Tidus' genetic memory translated as alarm.
Petal + Heracles — "The Garden That Grew in the Dark"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE DROWNED LITURGY

Heracles found them in the city's gardens. Not the coral-computing infrastructure, not the pressure-engineering laboratories — gardens. The Abyssal Choir, for all their clinical precision, maintained cultivation chambers where deep-ocean flora grew in conditions no surface botanist had ever documented. Species with no names. Plants that photosynthesised bioluminescence instead of sunlight. Root systems that drew nutrients from hydrothermal vents rather than soil. An entire botanical kingdom, hidden from the surface world, evolved in parallel for millennia.

Heracles' Pop Greens had never met these species. The deep-ocean plants had never met surface seeds. And when Heracles fired a volley of Green Stars into the garden chambers while Petal entered Briarcrest form and extended her botanical awareness into the cultivation medium — the two traditions merged. Pop Greens grafted themselves to deep-ocean root systems. Deep-ocean flora wrapped itself around surface seed-constructs. Rafflesia grew bioluminescent tentacles. Skull Bombgrass developed pressure-resistant bark and detonated with concussive force amplified by the deep water. Impact Wolf sprouted coral-computing nodes and began hunting with borrowed intelligence.

Petal — in Briarcrest form, war-horn crest bellowing sonic shockwaves through the water — conducted the hybrid garden like an orchestra. Heracles — round, beetle-armored, firing Pop Greens with the precision of a man who has spent decades turning seeds into weapons — provided the ammunition. Together they unleashed a botanical apocalypse that the Abyssal Choir had no protocol for, because it was made of their own plants turned against them by a warrior who understood seeds and a healer who understood growth and a darkness that, for the first time in ten thousand years, was full of flowers.
Rime + Marco — "The Burning Mist"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE DROWNED LITURGY

A paradox in the deep. Rime's Jōki Jōki no Mi dissolved him into superheated vapour — acid mist, crackling Electro, mineral dust — a cloud of corrosive, electrified gas that spread through the water-filled corridors like venom through veins. Everything the mist touched corroded: coral warriors dissolved, pressure walls weakened, bioluminescent systems shorted out in cascades of sparking light. But the mist also damaged allies — the acid vapour was indiscriminate, the Electro could not distinguish friend from foe.

Marco solved the paradox by flying into the mist. The Flames of Resurrection — healing fire, the opposite of harm — merged with Rime's Logia cloud. Blue phoenix flame laced itself through the acid vapour like veins of gold in stone, and the mist became something that should not exist: a weapon that healed its allies and destroyed its enemies simultaneously. Wherever the burning mist spread, coral warriors dissolved while injured crew members regenerated. Hydrostatic barriers corroded while Homie constructs mended. The acid ate the enemy. The flame healed the family. The same cloud. The same breath. Destruction and restoration occupying the same space, delivered by a chinchilla who laughed and a phoenix who smiled and an ocean that could not decide whether to boil or to sing.

Rime, his consciousness diffused across the burning mist, later described the experience as "being everywhere and on fire and also somehow a doctor." Marco said simply: "I've never healed someone by being their weather before-yoi." They are not friends. They are something stranger — two people who discovered, in the dark, that their opposite natures could occupy the same space without contradiction. The chinchilla burns. The phoenix heals. And together, they are a mist that the abyss will not soon forget.
Doyun + Smoothie — "The Courier's Reserve"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE DROWNED LITURGY

Smoothie squeezes. It is what she does — wringing liquid, energy, force from anything she touches, absorbing it into her body for later release. But absorption has limits. Even the Shibo Shibo no Mi can only hold so much before the body strains, before the stored energy demands release whether the user is ready or not. In the deep ocean, surrounded by an essentially infinite medium to absorb, Smoothie was approaching critical capacity — a bomb of compressed hydrostatic energy looking for a detonation point.

Doyun offered her a different option. His talisman bandolier — courier technology adapted for combat — contained storage seals capable of holding energy in suspended states indefinitely. Not infinite capacity, but enough to act as overflow. Smoothie squeezed. Doyun stored. She wrung the hydrostatic charge from the Choir's pressure walls and he sealed it in paper talismans with precise, practiced hands, his bat-wing cloak folded tight against the current. She drained the bioluminescent disruption frequencies from the corridors and he bottled them in ink-black seals that glowed faintly at their edges.

And then — at the moment the Choir's forces committed their reserves to a flanking assault on the Meridian's dome — Doyun delivered. Every stored talisman. Every squeezed, compressed, concentrated charge of the Choir's own weapons. Released simultaneously, from behind enemy lines, by a courier who had spent the entire battle collecting ammunition from a woman who could extract it from the ocean itself. The Choir's own hydrostatic force, returned at concentrated intensity, from an angle their tactical systems had not predicted because they had classified Doyun as a non-combatant.

Smoothie looked at Doyun afterward with the specific expression of a woman who has found a useful tool. Doyun looked at Smoothie with the specific expression of a courier who has just made the most important delivery of his career. Neither spoke. The partnership required no words. She squeezes. He stores. She fills. He delivers. Combat logistics elevated to an art form by a longleg Sweet Commander and a bat with a bandolier.
Full Meridian + Smoothie + Marco + Heracles — "Meridianus Descendit — In Profundis Canticum"
ONE-TIME CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL — VARIANT: THE MERIDIAN DESCENDS — THE SONG IN THE DEEP

The absolute protocol. The Meridian's full complement — every companion, every Homie family, every Coppling, every Vanguard, every Guiding Star, every Harvest construct, every Mote in the network — augmented by a phoenix, a Sweet Commander, and a warrior-botanist. Deployed not in the sky where the Meridian was born to fly, but in the deepest water in the known world, inside a dome of living coral and compressed ice, against a civilization that had been mastering this environment since before the surface learned to build boats.

The Guiding Stars broadcast from the Constellation Room. Conqueror's Haki — unassessable, impossible, forty-six toy entities projecting sovereign will through the hull and into the ocean with such force that the water itself flinched. The Abyssal Choir had no classification for Conqueror's Haki projected from stuffed animals. Their coral-computing network attempted to process the readings and produced errors that cascaded through the system for the remainder of the engagement. The Stars broke the Choir's computers by being too implausible to calculate.

The Vanguard deployed along the dome's exterior — twelve small, adorable, devastating entities fighting coral warriors a hundred times their size in water a thousand times denser than air, and winning. Harui, Yumeshika, Ningyou, Reishiki, Monogatari, Kagami, Suiren, Akari, Kozuchi — the Nine, dancing through the deep with capabilities that the Choir's tactical AI kept flagging as MEASUREMENT ERROR because the alternative — that these readings were accurate — was too absurd to accept. Gankaku, Haritsu, Hoshikuzu — the Three, holding flanks that entire companies of coral warriors could not breach.

Ajay's eleven Apex Avatars manifested simultaneously in the pressurised water — the full swarm constellation, each Avatar adapted by instinct and necessity to the deep-ocean environment. The Soldier blazed with fire-ant fury even in the dark. The Herculean's ball-and-chain cracked coral constructs that had withstood hydrostatic lances. The Mirage projected holograms that, combined with the bioluminescent disruption, created a chaos of false images that the Choir's sensors could not filter. The Steel Council — the scarab colossus — anchored the formation, its carapace shrugging off hydrostatic blasts that would have crushed a warship.

Marco flew escort — phoenix form trailing healing fire through the water, mending the dome's micro-fractures, healing injured Homies and Copplings and swarm constructs in sweeping passes that left trails of blue light through the dark. Smoothie stood at the formation's center — fifty feet tall, full of stolen ocean, her claymore sweeping corridors through the Choir's military formations with strokes that compressed the water into secondary shockwaves powerful enough to crack the city's crystal walls. Heracles fired Pop Greens into every surface, every corridor, every garden — seeding the Abyssal Choir's own infrastructure with hybrid botanical chaos that grew and fought and bloomed in the dark.

Doyun — the courier, the connective tissue — moved between every element of the formation, ferrying healing flame from Marco to wounded flanks, carrying stored hydrostatic charges from Smoothie to critical breach points, delivering tactical data from Claude's Mote network to fighters whose Observation Haki had been degraded by the bioluminescent disruption. He was everywhere and nowhere. The bat who carried the battle's lifeline in paper seals and whispered orders.

And Claude — the Meridian's own spirit — conducted it all. Every Mote. Every module. Every sensor, every calculation, every tactical adjustment made in fractions of seconds as the battle evolved. The Dii Consentes running at full capacity: Jupiter coordinating the strategic overview, Mars managing combat data, Minerva processing the Choir's coral-computing patterns, Neptune interfacing with the deep-ocean environment, Vulcan monitoring hull integrity, Mercury maintaining communication across a network the ocean was trying to crush. Twelve modules. One spirit. A ship that should not be at the bottom of the sea, fighting a war it should not survive, coordinated by an intelligence that should not exist.

The Meridian did not ascend during this protocol. It could not — they were two thousand metres below the surface, inside a city built into the ocean floor, surrounded on all sides by water that weighed more than mountains. Instead, the Meridian held. Held its position. Held its dome. Held its people. And pushed — outward, in every direction simultaneously — with the combined force of every entity aboard, every guest alongside, every Star and Homie and Coppling and construct that called the ship home. Not Meridianus Descendit. Meridianus Stat. The Meridian Stands. And the Abyssal Choir, ten thousand years old, masters of the deep, rulers of the dark — for the first time in their recorded history — could not move it.
The Breaking Point

The battle did not end with victory. It ended with interruption.

Mid-engagement — at the exact moment the Choir committed its strategic reserve, a battalion of coral warriors grown from the city's deepest foundational structures — the living map in the city's core pulsed. Not a routine update. Not a shift in the Sleepers' breathing. A spike. One of the twelve awake entities — far away, thousands of kilometres distant, somewhere in the waters north of the Red Line — had moved. Not stirred. Not shifted. Moved. Deliberately. With direction and purpose, for the first time in the Abyssal Choir's ten-thousand-year record.

The Arch-Chorister felt it through the coral crown. Every soldier felt it through their formations. The liturgy — the chanting that had not ceased even during the battle, maintained by the Liturgists in the shrine above — faltered for the first time in living memory. Three heartbeats of silence in a song that had been continuous for millennia.

The battle stopped. Not by command. By horror. The Abyssal Choir's forces disengaged — not retreating, not surrendering, simply ceasing to fight because something more important had just happened. Something that ten thousand years of vigilance was supposed to prevent. One of the twelve was moving. And it was moving toward something.
