I. The Gathering

Six weeks after Elbaf. The Mote network — still carrying the Sleeper's trace frequency like a splinter it could not remove — detected a second anomaly. Different. Where the Elbaf signal had been rhythmic, biological, alive, this one was torn. Not a pulse but a wound — a raw seam in the fabric of dimensional space that was leaking energy signatures Claude had only encountered once before: during the reconstruction of Ajay's transmigration data. The same flavour of impossible. The same taste of worlds that should not touch each other, touching.

The source: an unnamed island in the New World. No flag. No nation. No charted name. The kind of place that exists between territories — claimed by no one because there was nothing worth claiming. A volcanic atoll with dense forest, freshwater springs, and absolutely no strategic value. The sort of island the Log Pose skips. The sort of island that only appears on charts because someone once sailed past it and thought: "Unremarkable."

It was now crawling with Pre-Index organisms — pouring through a tear in reality itself — and five of the most dangerous people in the world had converged on it independently, each chasing the same impossible prey from different directions.

Dracule "Hawk Eyes" Mihawk — The World's Strongest Swordsman

He arrived first. Yoru on his back — the Supreme Grade black blade, one of twelve in the world, a sword that carries the permanent will of the man who turned it black through decades of combat that the pirate world considers legend. His cross-shaped pendant knife hung at his throat. Mihawk had been tracking the creatures for weeks. They had appeared in Cross Guild territory, dismantling outposts that even Marine Admirals hesitated to approach. Mihawk did not care about the outposts. He cared about what his hawk eyes had seen in the creatures' movements: a pattern. A direction. Every organism, regardless of where it surfaced, was orienting itself toward this island. Drawn by a signal too old for anyone else to perceive — but not too old for eyes that see everything.

Mihawk, who had spent a lifetime searching for the limit of what the blade could cut, had found something his flying slashes bounced off of. Not because the creatures were harder than steel. Because they were so ancient that the concept of being cut might not fully apply to them. His blade — Yoru, the night itself — had responded to these creatures differently than to anything else it had ever struck. As if the sword recognized something it did not want to name.

The World's Strongest Swordsman stood on the shore of an island no one had ever cared about, watching things crawl out of a hole in reality, and for the first time in memory, he was not bored. He was interested.
Charlotte Smoothie — Sweet Commander of the Big Mom Pirates

She arrived second. The tallest woman most pirates would ever see — a longleg-human hybrid carrying a claymore longer than most people are tall and a bounty of ₿932,000,000. The only Sweet Commander never defeated by a Super Rookie. The Minister of Juice who governs 100% Island in what remains of Totto Land's territorial claim.

Big Mom was gone. The empire was fracturing. But Smoothie's assignment had come from Katakuri himself — the creatures had been appearing near Totto Land's borders, and they were doing something that Smoothie recognized immediately: they were draining the islands. Not eating. Not destroying. Draining. Wringing the life from the land the way her own Shibo Shibo no Mi wrings moisture from flesh. She recognized the mechanism because it mirrored her Devil Fruit's function at a geological scale, and that recognition disturbed her more than the creatures themselves. Whatever these things were, they operated on the same principle as her fruit — extraction, compression, consumption — except they did it to terrain.

Smoothie does not rattle easily. She has stood in the presence of Big Mom's hunger rampages and kept her composure. She has wrung the fluid from enemies without blinking. But watching something do to an island what she does to a person — watching the earth shrivel — gave her a pause that her subordinates had never witnessed.
Perona — The Ghost Princess

She arrived with Mihawk, as she often does. The Ghost Princess — formerly of Thriller Bark, keeper of the Horo Horo no Mi — had her own reasons for following. Her Negative Hollows had been behaving strangely for weeks. Normally, a Negative Hollow passes through a target and fills them with crippling self-loathing — a depression so total that even the strongest pirates collapse into weeping puddles of despair. It bypasses all physical defenses. It does not care about Haki, about willpower, about resolve. It targets the architecture of selfhood, and there has never been a target immune to it except one: Usopp, whose own depression was already so profound that the hollows found nothing to add.

Until now. Perona's hollows passed through the Pre-Index creatures and found — nothing. Not resistance. Not immunity. Absence. There was nothing inside these organisms for the hollows to target. No emotion. No self-worth. No self at all. These things predated the concept of feeling bad about oneself. They predated the concept of feeling. The hollows entered and exited like light through glass, carrying nothing, affecting nothing, reporting back to Perona with the spectral equivalent of a confused shrug.

This terrified her. Not because she was defenseless — her explosive Mini Hollows and Toku Hollows still functioned as conventional artillery. But because she had never encountered anything that simply didn't have what her fruit needed to work. It was like discovering that a key she had trusted her entire life did not fit any lock on the island, because the locks predated the invention of keyholes.

Her ghost surveillance network, however, was invaluable. Dozens of spectral scouts scattered across the island, invisible, intangible, mapping every creature's position in real time. Perona's combat utility might have been diminished, but her intelligence value was peerless.
Kawamatsu the Kappa — Red Scabbard of Wano

He came from Wano. A nine-foot-tall pufferfish fish-man who had spent twenty years pretending to be a kappa — at his mother's dying instruction, because Wano's isolationism made it dangerous to be visibly different. He carried Soto Muso, practised Kappa-ryu — a swordsmanship that blends the blade with sumo wrestling — and bore the loyalty of a Red Scabbard, one of Kozuki Oden's nine retainers. Kawamatsu had survived thirteen years of imprisonment eating poisoned fish bones. He had seen his lord burned alive and smiled through the flames. He had believed, for two decades, that he knew what monsters looked like.

He was wrong.

The creatures had appeared at Wano's barely-opened borders. The nation had been isolationist for generations, its ports just now cracking open under Momonosuke's young reign. And the first things that came through the door were organisms that made Sea Kings look like koi fish. Kawamatsu was sent because a samurai was needed — someone who could fight what the common soldiers could not — and because his fish-man physiology gave him advantages in environments where these creatures seemed to thrive: deep water, crushing pressure, the boundary between land and sea.

He carried with him the Ryou of Wano — the advanced emission-type Armament Haki that the samurai call "flowing." The Haki that does not coat the blade but passes through the target. The kind of Haki that could, if pushed, hurt things that ordinary cutting could not reach.
Admiral Fujitora — Issho

He arrived last. The blind Marine Admiral walked off a Navy battleship with his cane-sword — Yakuza Kasen, a Supreme Grade blade disguised as a walking stick — tapping the ground in measured beats. No eyes. No sight. His Observation Haki painted the world in a resolution that made vision seem crude by comparison. Every creature on the island, every heartbeat, every shift in mass and pressure — Fujitora perceived it all through senses that did not require light.

Issho followed "Humane Justice" — the only Marine doctrine that places civilian lives above orders, above rank, above the institution itself. He had kneeled before civilians in Dressrosa. He had defied the World Government when obedience would have cost innocent lives. He was sent to this island because the Marines classified the dimensional anomaly as a Buster Call-level threat, and they sent their most principled Admiral — the one least likely to destroy the island along with the creatures.

But Fujitora had a deeper reason. His Zushi Zushi no Mi — the Press-Press Fruit — allowed him to generate and manipulate gravitational forces. He could pull meteors from space. He could crush warships into the sea floor. He could bend the trajectory of light itself. And the rift on this island was producing gravitational distortions that made his teeth ache. Space was not supposed to fold like this. Mass was not supposed to behave like this. The universe's own laws were being violated, and Fujitora could feel every violation through his fruit like static on his skin. Whatever had torn this hole in reality had done so by applying force in a direction that gravity did not have — and the blind admiral, whose entire combat doctrine was built on the absolute reliability of gravitational law, found himself standing before evidence that the law had an exception.
The Accidental Assembly

The Meridian arrived to find all five of them already fighting. Separately. Mihawk on the northern shore, his flying slashes carving corridors through creatures that closed behind him like water. Smoothie on the eastern cliffs, grown enormous from absorbing alien fluids, her claymore sweeping. Perona hovering above the treeline in astral projection, her physical body hidden somewhere safe, her ghosts detonating across the canopy. Kawamatsu in the shallows, Kappa-ryu shockwaves sending geysers of water and fur into the air. Fujitora in the island's interior, his gravity fields pinning creatures to the ground while his cane-sword cut them apart in precise, economical strokes.

Five people who had no reason to be in the same place. Five factions — Cross Guild, Big Mom Pirates, Thriller Bark's remnant, Wano's samurai, and the Marines — that were, under any normal circumstance, enemies. And a flying ship crewed by the most wanted pirate in the Sovereign tier, his three companions, forty Homies, eight Copplings, twelve Vanguard, forty-six Guiding Stars, twelve Harvest constructs, one Conductor, one Jelly Princess, and the ship's own spirit.

Nobody proposed an alliance. Nobody needed to. The creatures were pouring through the rift faster than any single party could contain, and the island was shrinking as the organisms drained it. The trees were withering. The springs were drying. The volcanic rock was turning to dust. The math was simple: fight together or die separately.

Mihawk made eye contact with Ajay once. One long, evaluating look from the eyes that see everything, at the boy with the golden grin and the swarm that filled the sky. Whatever he saw, he nodded — once — and returned to cutting. That nod was the alliance.
